# SEID 2364 Big Ideas Framework

## Purpose

This document isolates the Big Ideas that frame SEID 2364 so they can be ordered, refined, and aligned with the course’s developmental arc. The goal is not only to preserve the original Lecture 1 ideas, but to determine how they function across the semester as recurring conceptual anchors. The Big Ideas are introduced early, but each is revisited in greater depth as the course progresses through ethics, mediation, data science systems, complexity, future capability, and professional responsibility.

This document is provisional. The set of Big Ideas may still evolve.

## Larger Areas of the Framework

The Big Ideas can also be understood in four larger developmental areas:

### 1. Foundational Conditions for Ethical Need
**Big Ideas 1–3** establish the minimum conditions under which ethical need becomes practically relevant: more than one intelligence, some form of connection, and the ability to effect consequential change.

### 2. Context, Frameworks, and Moral Interpretation
**Big Ideas 4–5** move from foundational conditions into interpretive complexity: context alters judgment, and ethical systems must balance universal and cultural claims.

### 3. Machine Systems, Structural Limits, and Time
**Big Ideas 6–9** shift the discussion more explicitly into data science and socio-technical systems: machine intelligence becomes ethically relevant, engineered systems encounter structural limits, ideas and technologies become historically irreversible, and ethical design must anticipate future capability and change.

### 4. Complexity, Wicked Problems, and Professional Responsibility
**Big Idea 10** culminates the sequence by focusing on the most ethically complex socio-technical problems and the heightened responsibility of data scientists within a computational society.

## Rationale for Ordering

The Big Ideas should be ordered not simply by their original lecture appearance, but by how the course develops them over time. The order should support:

1. early conceptual accessibility,
2. growing ethical complexity,
3. increasing relevance to data science and machine systems,
4. later-semester stress testing through wicked problems, future capability, and institutional responsibility.

## Working Ordered List

### Big Idea 1. More Than One Intelligence

The need for ethics emerges where there is more than one intelligence in a relation of possible consequence. The claim here is not that ethics does not exist as an idea in isolation, but that the practical need for an ethical framework arises when one intelligence may matter to another.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Aristotle, Martin Buber, Emmanuel Levinas, Smith/Bianchi (BBS).

### Big Idea 2. Connected Intelligences

The need for ethics becomes relevant only when intelligences are connected through some pathway of mediation. If intelligences are completely isolated and cannot reach or affect one another, no ethical framework between them is required. In human and data-driven systems, connection may be direct or mediated, local or remote, synchronous or asynchronous, and it may be unidirectional or bidirectional. Each pathway, and each direction within a pathway, may differ in visibility, delay, accessibility, authority, and consequence.

Connection is also conditioned by observability. Intelligences may be directly visible to one another, partially observable, delayed, inferred, or hidden behind layers of mediation. This opens the way to relativistic thinking: what one intelligence can know about another depends on position, pathway, accessibility, and delay. It also opens the way to a more quantum-like problem: ethical speculation can outrun grounded ethical judgment when the relevant state of the system cannot yet be observed. If the condition requiring judgment is unresolved, inaccessible, or effectively in superposition from the observer’s point of view, then ethical analysis remains provisional. In human and computational systems alike, ethical relevance is shaped not only by whether a connection exists, but by how that connection can be observed.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Claude Shannon, Norbert Wiener, Albert Einstein, Erwin Schrödinger, Marshall McLuhan, Smith/Bianchi (BBS).

### Big Idea 3. The Ability to Effect Change

Ethical judgment becomes necessary when an intelligence, agent, or system can effect consequential change in a shared or mediated space. Without the ability to alter the state of the relevant environment, the need for ethical judgment does not arise in the same way. This idea is about consequence in the ordinary sense of effect and change, not consequentialism as a formal moral theory.

This also introduces a crucial bridge to the next idea: in some systems, the act of observation is not neutral. To observe, classify, measure, or monitor may already be to alter the state of the environment. Once observation itself becomes intervention, ethical judgment becomes even more dependent on context.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Norbert Wiener, Werner Heisenberg, Smith/Bianchi (BBS).

### Big Idea 4. Context Changes Ethical Judgment

The same behavior in different situations may be judged differently. Intent, scale, power, role, knowledge, institutional setting, and consequence all reshape moral evaluation. Some situations remain relatively ethically obvious across contexts, while others become unstable as context thickens and values come into tension.

In these more complex situations, there may be no single obviously best solution. Different interpretations of the same event may be equally or at least plausibly defensible, and different responses may carry different forms of moral residue or loss. Different ethical frameworks may therefore justify different conclusions from the same case. This becomes the bridge to the next idea: once context destabilizes judgment and multiple interpretations become defensible, we must ask whether ethics is always contextual, or whether some principles remain universal despite contextual variation.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Aristotle (phronesis), Joseph Fletcher, Erving Goffman, contemporary case-based ethics.

### Big Idea 5. Balancing Universal and Cultural Morality

An important question for the course is whether there are any ethical principles that can be considered truly universal. Some ethical systems claim that certain principles hold regardless of context, while others treat moral judgment as historically, culturally, or situationally conditioned. The course does not assume in advance that contextual variation eliminates universality; instead, it asks how claims to universality are grounded, defended, and tested across different frameworks and situations.

Claims of universality do not arise from a single source. They may emerge from religion, philosophy, law, human rights traditions, or professional codes, each grounded in different axiomatic assumptions about duty, consequence, virtue, care, justice, or rights. Entire societies can be shaped by these frameworks, and they often function as large-scale structures of obligation, prohibition, legitimacy, and moral expectation. Here, culture is not limited to society or civilization alone. It may also refer to religion, organization, profession, institution, or other overlapping moral communities.

At the same time, technological change, pluralism, and complex mediated systems place these frameworks into contact and tension. A principle that appears universal in one setting may be interpreted differently in another. In practice, many institutions are not governed by a single pure framework. They often justify one kind of rule through another kind of framework, using consequence- and risk-based reasoning to generate deontic rules, obligations, and prohibitions.

This means that “universal versus cultural” is not a simple binary. The deeper question is how different axiomatic systems generate different claims to universality, and how those claims persist, conflict, or hybridize within real societies and technological systems. This also returns students to their own ethical development: when asked to formulate a personal ethical framework, many discover that they do in fact hold certain principles as non-negotiable, even across professional or institutional pressure.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Kant, Ruth Benedict, Martha Nussbaum, religious moral traditions, human rights discourse, comparative ethics.

### Big Idea 6. Intelligent Machines Need Ethics Too

Machine intelligence does not need to be human in order to become ethically relevant. Once machine systems participate in environments where they can classify, prioritize, predict, recommend, allocate, monitor, or otherwise affect people and institutions, they enter ethical systems. Their outputs influence action, distribute opportunity and harm, and can scale decisions far beyond the reach of individual human judgment. For this reason, machine intelligence is not ethically neutral simply because it is artificial.

This is especially important for data science because data scientists increasingly design, tune, and deploy the systems through which machine behavior is framed. As machine intelligence becomes more probabilistic, adaptive, and generative, we move away from systems whose outputs can be fully guaranteed in advance through formal operations alone. The systems we build around machine intelligence also become more complex: more layered, more distributed, more interdependent, and harder to fully predict or govern from any single point of control. Ethical design therefore shifts from specifying exact outcomes to constructing architectures of framing, constraint, monitoring, and revision. Guardrails are one example of this shift: they do not eliminate ethical risk, but attempt to shape a space of possible behavior, even as users and systems may discover ways around them. This increasing complexity also foreshadows a later course question: how should we design ethically for future capabilities that may exceed the assumptions of the present?

This does not mean that machines possess morality in the same way humans do. It means that human–machine systems require ethical architecture, and that responsibility for machine behavior is often distributed rather than singular. Unlike human behavior, which can often be assigned to an individual actor and judged accordingly, machine behavior emerges through hybrid systems of designers, organizations, data, interfaces, infrastructures, incentives, and users. The challenge is not whether responsibility exists, but how it is distributed, obscured, and governed.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Alan Turing, Norbert Wiener, Isaac Asimov, Luciano Floridi, ACM/IEEE ethics frameworks.

### Big Idea 7. Systems Have Structural Limits

Some ethical failures may result from bugs, while others arise from limitations of local implementation, data quality, or engineering context. But no matter how well we design a system, we still encounter limits that are structural rather than accidental—limits that remain even in the best engineered systems. No sufficiently expressive formal system resolves every conflict from within itself. Some questions may be structurally undecidable, some truths may be unprovable within the system that contains them, and some self-referential statements may resist stable resolution altogether. Gödel, Turing, Church, the halting problem, and classical paradoxes all point toward the same deeper lesson: formal systems are powerful, but not complete.

This matters for data science and ethics because computational systems inherit these limits rather than escaping them. The act of formalizing a problem does not guarantee that every important question can be resolved by rule, proof, optimization, or code. A bug is not the same as a structural limit, though it may function as a limitation in practice. Structural limits remain even when the system is well designed.

Some limits are formal and mathematical, such as unprovability, undecidability, and representational bounds. Others arise from mediation itself: incomplete data, inaccessible states, delayed information, limited trust, partial observability, and the fact that distance may restrict what a system can know in time to act. In BBS terms, some limits are not only inside the formal structure, but in the availability, trustworthiness, and completeness of the data that can be obtained across mediated space.

This places an important boundary on the dream of total ethical automation. Computation can clarify, scale, simulate, and support judgment, but it cannot guarantee that every morally relevant problem will collapse into a single provable or computable answer. Good engineering often means designing intelligently within limits rather than imagining that the limits can be abolished.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Kurt Gödel, Alan Turing, Alonzo Church, Claude Shannon, liar paradox / self-reference traditions.

### Big Idea 8. Technology and Ideas Are Irreversible

Pandora’s Box is a useful metaphor for technological and conceptual release. Once an analytical capability, technical artifact, or structural idea is opened into a socio-technical system, it cannot simply be returned to a pre-release state. The introduction itself becomes historical. Time introduces a condition different from space: objects, systems, and data may sometimes be moved, isolated, reconfigured, or even deleted, but the fact of introduction moves only forward. Once something has entered history, later states of the world inherit that event even if the original artifact is altered, lost, or removed. But irreversibility does not imply inevitability. Some ideas replicate, scale, and become embedded because they function within a broader material and institutional ecology. Others fail, are selected against, or remain dormant because they arrive too early, lack viability, or cannot secure the support needed to persist.

Ideas that work often become difficult to remove because they integrate into infrastructures, expectations, workflows, markets, institutions, and habits of thought. Yet even this embedding is not absolute. Some ideas survive only as latent technical DNA that later reappears in new forms when conditions change. Others may be valid in principle but disappear through neglect, disaster, infrastructural fragility, or the loss of the systems needed to preserve them. What matters, then, is not only whether something has been introduced, but how it survives, propagates, disappears, or re-emerges across time.

The survival of an idea is shaped not only by technical merit, but also by power, social closure, institutional filtering, and the conditions under which a system can be maintained. This means that BI8 is not a simple story of linear progress. Once the box is opened, history cannot be reset, but the futures that follow may include embedding, latency, extinction, recovery, or collapse.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Pandora myth, Thomas P. Hughes, Pinch & Bijker (SCOT, critically), W. Brian Arthur, Jared Diamond, Virtual Orchestra / BBS.

### Big Idea 9. Plan for Future Capabilities

Ethical design should not be limited to present capabilities alone. Systems should be judged not only by what they do now, but by what they may later become when they scale, accelerate, cheapen, hybridize, or become embedded in larger infrastructures. A design that appears acceptable under current technical conditions may become ethically inadequate when future capabilities alter what can be inferred, automated, surveilled, broken, or weaponized.

This means that data scientists must learn to design for trajectories rather than snapshots. The question is not only whether a system is safe, fair, or responsible today, but how its ethical meaning may change when it is integrated into broader systems, used by actors with different incentives, or exposed to new technical conditions. Ethical responsibility therefore includes anticipation: thinking through how a present design decision may be reinterpreted under future capability shifts. No matter how well we anticipate the future, other changes outside the system will also occur. Cultural, political, legal, economic, and scientific shifts can alter the ethical meaning of a system just as profoundly as technical change within it. For this reason, the systems we design will not only continue to change; they will need to change.

Future capability does not imply simple or endless progress. Some systems may continue to expand, some may stabilize, and others may enter cycles of fragility, crisis, or collapse. Present safeguards may not remain durable under these shifts. In this sense, ethical design must address not only present performance, but future reinterpretation, future misuse, and future breakdown.

This also opens a later question for the course: if machine intelligence becomes more autonomous, adaptive, persistent, and socially embedded, will there come a time when it is itself treated as responsible for its actions rather than only as an object of human and institutional responsibility? For now, this remains an open future-facing problem rather than a settled claim.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Thomas P. Hughes, W. Brian Arthur, Arc of Engineering, Red Queen metaphor, Jared Diamond.

### Big Idea 10. Ethics Becomes Most Important When Problems Are Most Complex

Some ethical problems are relatively unambiguous and require little extended analysis. Others become difficult because stakeholders disagree, information is incomplete, values conflict, and every available option carries cost. The most ethically interesting problems are often those in which no solution is clean, no perspective is complete, and every intervention changes the conditions of the problem itself.

Complex socio-technical systems intensify this difficulty. Data-driven environments often mediate decisions across multiple agents, institutions, and timescales, making harms harder to see, responsibility harder to assign, and consequences harder to predict. The most ethically important problems are therefore often the least reducible to simple rules or single metrics. They require judgment under uncertainty, reflection across competing frameworks, and a willingness to revisit conclusions as systems evolve.

In a computational society, data scientists bear heightened responsibility because they increasingly design, tune, and maintain the systems through which these difficult decisions are structured, operationalized, and scaled. Their work does not merely analyze the world; it participates in shaping the environments within which ethical life unfolds. For this reason, the more complex the problem, the greater the need for disciplined ethical reasoning rather than narrow technical optimization.

Some of the most ethically complex problems we encounter have been classified as Wicked Problems. A Wicked Problem is not simply a hard problem; it is a problem whose boundaries, stakeholders, consequences, and even definitions are contested, unstable, or changing. Such problems resist final resolution because attempts to solve them often transform the system in which they occur. In these cases, ethical reasoning is not a decorative supplement to technical design; it becomes essential to responsible action.

**Possible contributors / touchstones:** Rittel & Webber, Norbert Wiener, Luciano Floridi, Cathy O’Neil, Safiya Noble, professional codes in computing and engineering.

## Use in the Course

These Big Ideas are introduced early, but they are not exhausted in the first class. They recur across provocations, case studies, games, discussions, readings, BBS/EDOCA analysis, AI-use reflection, and the final ethical framework. The course should therefore be designed so that students move from initial recognition of the Big Ideas toward increasingly explicit, disciplined, and applied engagement with them over time.
