# SEID 2364 Course Assessment and Modification Design Document  
## Integrated Framing Version for Course Redesign

## Purpose

This document summarizes the proposed modifications for the next offering of SEID 2364: *Societal and Ethical Impacts of Data Science*. The first delivery of the course functioned as a live pilot in which course structure, assignments, delivery modalities, technical infrastructure, and ethical framing were developed in real time. The course did not fail; rather, it revealed the expected instability of a first implementation built around emerging materials, a hybrid international teaching schedule, and an evolving framework for mediated ethical reasoning.

The next iteration should preserve the course’s experimental and reflective character while replacing just-in-time invention with a more stable operating architecture. The goal is not to make the course rigid. The goal is to pre-build enough modular structure that adaptation becomes pedagogically intentional rather than logistically reactive.

This revision also clarifies the deeper orientation of the course: students are not only learning to evaluate present-day data science systems. They are learning to reason ethically about systems whose future capabilities may exceed current legal, institutional, or cultural expectations. Current regulatory frameworks such as the EU AI Act remain essential reference points, but ethical preparation cannot stop at compliance. Students must learn to anticipate how data-driven and machine-intelligence systems may become more ambient, personalized, autonomous, multimodal, and institutionally embedded. The course therefore trains students to reason responsibly under conditions of uncertainty, ambiguity, acceleration, and future capability expansion.

## 1. General Assessment of the First Iteration

The first offering demonstrated that the course has a strong conceptual foundation. The framing of ethics as practice within mediated socio-technical environments is viable, and students can engage meaningfully with data science ethics when ethical problems are treated as ambiguous, situated, and shaped by systems rather than as simple applications of rules.

Several design challenges emerged. Attendance was difficult to assess organically because the course intentionally avoided mechanical roll-call procedures. The private journal system supported reflection, but its flexibility made it difficult to use as a consistent participation artifact. GitHub and Zotero were valuable but were not sufficiently enforced from the beginning. Asynchronous activities were useful, but they required clearer feedback loops and more structured student-to-student interaction. The final assignment existed, but it needs to become more explicit as the organizing arc of the course from the first day.

The next course version should therefore focus on four broad improvements: clearer engagement evidence, stronger recurring assignment scaffolding, more intentional use of delivery modalities, and better integration of repository-based workflows.

The redesign should also sharpen the course’s future-facing ethical purpose. Students should not be trained only to identify whether a system is currently legal, currently acceptable, or currently technically possible. They should learn to ask what happens when a present capability scales, becomes cheaper, becomes personalized, becomes automated, or becomes embedded invisibly into ordinary life. In this sense, the course prepares students for ethical judgment in a moving technological landscape rather than for static rule application.

## 2. Course Operating Principle

The course should be redesigned around a repeatable pattern that appears in most class sessions:

Students begin with a provocation. They encounter or revisit a concept. They engage in a case, activity, game, or mediated environment. They analyze that experience using relevant ethical frameworks, BBS, EDOCA, or another descriptive tool. They perform a small evidence-based action through Zotero. They leave a trace in the course repository or discussion environment. They revise some part of their developing ethical framework.

This pattern creates continuity across face-to-face, synchronous remote, and asynchronous activities. It also allows each class to contribute to the final assignment without requiring every class to have the same instructional form.

To make this rhythm more explicit, the next version of the course should use SRDMPA as an underlying project-management and learning-cycle framework:

**Speculate.** Students begin with a provocation, scenario, ambiguity, or wicked problem. They capture their first ethical intuition and identify what they think is at stake.

**Research.** Students test that first intuition through source-gathering, evidence review, precedent analysis, policy comparison, or technical investigation. Zotero becomes the recurring evidence infrastructure for this stage.

**Design.** Students use BBS, EDOCA, stakeholder analysis, ethical frameworks, or related tools to model the situation. They identify agents, spaces, assumptions, pathways, institutions, risks, values, and possible interventions.

**Make/Produce.** Students create an artifact: a case note, BBS map, EDOCA diagnostic, repository entry, team draft, AI-use trace, simulation response, policy memo, or ethical framework revision.

**Publish/Present.** Students make some version of their work visible to others through GitHub, the course wiki, a class presentation, peer review, group discussion, or a shared repository trace.

**Assess.** Students test the artifact against evidence, peer critique, AI-generated counterarguments, professional codes, ethical frameworks, stakeholder consequences, and their own developing professional commitments.

SRDMPA should not turn the course into a rigid engineering sprint. Rather, it should make visible the iterative structure already implicit in ethical learning: initial judgment, evidence, design, action, exposure, feedback, and revision.

## 3. Attendance and Engagement

Attendance should be assessed through organic participation artifacts rather than through roll call. The preferred mechanism is a short opening provocation submitted through a standard platform. This preserves the reflective function of the course while giving the instructor reliable evidence of participation.

The provocation should be low-stakes and normally graded complete/incomplete. Its purpose is to support attendance, retrieval, ethical reflection, and concept signaling. As in the instructor’s earlier five-minute quiz model, the provocation can indicate which concepts are structurally important and likely to reappear in higher-stakes assignments.

The private journal should remain private. However, students may periodically submit selected journal-derived reflections, excerpts, or structured responses. The distinction should be explicit: the private journal is for thinking; the submitted provocation is a course artifact.

Provocations should also serve the “Speculate” phase of SRDMPA. They should not merely record attendance; they should capture the student’s first ethical position before research, analysis, or peer discussion modifies that position.

## 4. Final Assignment as Week 1 Anchor

The final assignment should be introduced on the first day of class. Students should complete an initial low-stakes version during the first class session, most likely as the first provocation.

A possible Week 1 prompt:

“Given what you know today, describe the ethical framework you would use as a data scientist working with human, institutional, and machine intelligences. What values would guide your decisions, and what kinds of situations would put those values under pressure?”

The final assignment then becomes a return to this first answer. Students revise their initial framework using the course’s cases, ethical theories, BBS/EDOCA analysis, team experiences, AI-use reflections, Zotero-supported evidence, game-based simulations, peer critique, and modality-based activities.

The final project should therefore be framed not as a new task at the end of the semester, but as the mature version of the first ethical claim students make in the course. This structure makes ethical growth visible. Students are assessed not only on final polish, but on their ability to revise judgment in response to evidence, complexity, contradiction, and experience.

## 5. GitHub as the Course Environment

The GitHub repository and wiki have already been created and should now be updated rather than built from scratch. The repository should function as the course operating system. It should not merely store files; it should structure student behavior, documentation, feedback, and shared responsibility.

The repository template should include updated versions of the following:

- Provocation submission structure.
- Weekly assignment templates.
- AI-use disclosure and decision-trace template.
- BBS/EDOCA mapping template.
- Zotero-use instructions per assignment.
- Discussion and peer-feedback protocols.
- Team process reflection template.
- Game/simulation reflection template.
- Final project checkpoint structure.
- Synchronous remote participation rules.
- Asynchronous activity expectations.

GitHub should also be treated as an ethical learning environment. Students are not only learning repository management; they are inhabiting a system of shared records, visibility, authority, contribution, revision, and accountability.

In the next version of the course, GitHub should function as a kind of course flight data recorder. It should capture not only final submissions, but also decisions, changes, revisions, sources, AI interactions, peer feedback, and process history. This is especially important in a course concerned with responsibility, mediation, and accountability. Ethical reasoning should leave a trace.

Each major artifact should include an AI-use and decision trace. Students should document whether AI was used, what role it played, which suggestions or outputs were accepted, which were rejected, and why. This shifts AI disclosure from a compliance statement into a reflective account of design judgment.

## 7. Zotero as Evidence Infrastructure

Zotero should be required from the beginning and used in every class or assignment cycle. The activity already requires Zotero use, and the next course should make this expectation explicit.

Zotero is not simply a citation tool. In this course, it should function as the evidence infrastructure for ethical reasoning. Students should learn that ethical claims about data science require sources, evidence, context, and attribution.

Possible recurring Zotero actions include adding a source, annotating a source, connecting a source to an ethical claim, comparing conflicting sources, exporting a bibliography, identifying weak evidence, or revising a claim after source review.

Zotero should also function as an anti-hallucination check within AI-supported coursework. AI may support exploration, synthesis, and counterargument generation, but claims that enter assessed work should be anchored to traceable sources. Zotero does not guarantee truth, but it creates a disciplined evidence trail. It helps distinguish speculation from supported argument, and it gives students a practical method for managing the difference between AI-generated plausibility and verifiable evidence.

## 8. Asynchronous Learning as Iterative Ethical Mediation

Asynchronous work should be redesigned as a major modality of the course, not as homework overflow. The first iteration required rapid invention after each class. In the next version, many asynchronous structures can be prepared in advance and refined rather than created under pressure.

Asynchronous activities should include multiple categories:

**Reflective asynchronous work:** private journals, submitted provocations, ethical framework revisions, and AI-use reflections.

**Dialogic asynchronous work:** discussion boards, GitHub Discussions, structured disagreement, and ethical debate threads.

**Collaborative asynchronous work:** shared project documents, repository issues, Zotero libraries, wiki contributions, and group BBS/EDOCA maps.

**Evaluative asynchronous work:** peer review, stress-testing, challenge-response cycles, and rubric-based critique.

**Archival asynchronous work:** version histories, AI-use logs, bibliographies, and repository traces.

**AI-mediated asynchronous work:** AI-generated counterarguments, stakeholder simulations, summaries, and critique documents, always followed by human evaluation.

Each asynchronous activity must have a return path. That return path may be instructor feedback, peer response, group revision, AI-assisted synthesis, class discussion, or incorporation into a later assignment. Without a return path, asynchronous work becomes a dead drop rather than a meaningful mediation pathway.

The SRDMPA cycle should help structure these return paths. Asynchronous work should normally indicate which stage it serves: speculation, research, design, production, publication, or assessment.

## 9. Synchronous Remote Rules of Engagement

Because approximately half the course is remote, synchronous remote participation must have explicit rules. The ethical issue is not simply whether cameras are on or off. The issue is whether all participants understand the communicative game being played.

A camera-on seminar, an audio-first seminar, a chat-supported seminar, and a shared-document workshop are different ethical environments. Each has different assumptions about visibility, attention, power, access, and accountability.

The course should define acceptable synchronous modes. Students should know whether a given session depends on visual presence, voice participation, chat interaction, collaborative writing, or some combination. This avoids the mismatch in which the instructor is forced to operate in a different game state from the students.

These remote rules should be introduced as course infrastructure and as course content. Students should understand that mediated participation is not neutral. The class’s own communication systems become examples of the ethical environments being studied.

## 10. Residency-Anchor Delivery Model

The course should be designed around the fixed reality of international commuting. The hybrid structure is not a compromise; it is a core design constraint and a subject of ethical analysis.

The delivery model should be organized as follows:

**Opening Vienna Residency:** approximately two weeks face-to-face. This period should establish the course ecology, introduce GitHub and Zotero, present the final assignment, initiate the provocation system, form teams, introduce BBS/EDOCA vocabulary, and begin embodied or game-based ethical activities.

**Remote Block I:** begins synchronously to preserve continuity after the opening residency, then gradually introduces structured asynchronous strategies. This block should reinforce expectations and transition students into discussion boards, GitHub issues, Zotero evidence work, peer stress-testing, and AI-use documentation.

**Mid-Semester Vienna Residency:** ideally scheduled during City Tech spring break when Modul remains in session. One week can serve as a compression intensive. Two weeks would allow a stronger structure: one week for embodied simulations, games, and BBS/EDOCA workshops; a second week for project revision, peer critique, and applied synthesis.

**Remote Block II:** focuses on advanced asynchronous work, project development, peer stress-testing, evidence revision, and preparation for final synthesis.

**Final Vienna Residency:** the final month should emphasize application, presentation, defense, revision, assessment, and possible public or semi-public dissemination. By this stage, the core conceptual material should largely be in place.

Each modality should do what it does best. Face-to-face work should emphasize embodied interaction, trust, games, conflict, improvisation, and synthesis. Synchronous remote work should emphasize continuity, alignment, and live interpretation. Asynchronous work should emphasize reflection, evidence-building, peer challenge, documentation, and ethical traceability.

## 11. Game-Based Simulations and Game Theory

Game-based simulations should be introduced earlier and treated as a major course modality. Games are valuable because they make rules, incentives, agency, trust, and harm visible. They allow students to experience ethical systems rather than only discuss them.

Games also provide a useful bridge to BBS and EDOCA. A game contains agents, rules, spaces, signals, allowable moves, hidden information, permitted strategies, forbidden actions, and consequences.

One particularly useful distinction is in-system versus extra-system cheating.

**In-system cheating** occurs when a participant exploits the rules while technically remaining within them. Examples include bluffing, strategic ambiguity, withholding information, exploiting loopholes, or optimizing against the spirit of the system while obeying its formal constraints.

**Extra-system cheating** occurs when a participant violates the rule environment itself. Examples include secretly changing scores, looking at hidden information, using unauthorized tools, falsifying records, or coordinating through prohibited channels.

This distinction maps well to data science ethics. Some harmful systems are legally or formally permitted but ethically corrosive. Others involve direct violation, fraud, or unauthorized action. Students can use games to understand how ethical failure may arise both inside and outside formal rule structures.

Game-based work should also help students recognize that interpretive flexibility does not occur in a neutral environment. Social Construction of Technology and related approaches can be useful for showing how technologies acquire meaning through social negotiation, but this should remain a modifying lens rather than the central framework of the course. In data science systems, market pressure, institutional authority, cost-efficiency, infrastructure lock-in, platform control, and asymmetrical access often foreclose open negotiation long before all stakeholders can participate.

Students should therefore be asked not only “What do different groups believe this technology means?” but also “Who has the power to close the debate, define the rules, control the platform, or make one interpretation operational?” This distinction is especially important when studying algorithmic governance, platform moderation, recommender systems, predictive analytics, and AI policy.

The course may also introduce the idea that discarded or resisted technical forms do not always disappear. Their structural logic can reappear later under new technical conditions. This “latent technical DNA” concept can help students see technological history as a reservoir of possible futures rather than a simple sequence of obsolete and successful systems.

## 12. Team-Based Projects and Team Ethics

The first iteration had only five students, with possible attrition to three. That produced a tutorial-like environment, useful for discussion but limited as a laboratory for distributed responsibility. If future enrollment lands closer to fifteen, the course can more fully use teams as ethical systems.

The course should be designed to scale across enrollment bands:

For 3–5 students, the class functions as a studio cohort. Students rotate roles such as project lead, respondent, skeptic, documentation steward, Zotero/evidence steward, and AI-use auditor.

For 6–10 students, the course can use pairs or triads with cross-review and rotating stress-test responsibilities.

For 12–18 students, the course should use three to five teams. This is the ideal range for team-based ethical analysis.

The team itself should become an object of ethical analysis. Each team should produce not only an external case study but also a short internal process reflection.

The team process reflection should address responsibility, authority, trust, communication, harm, attribution, AI use, evidence management, and revision. Students should analyze whether their team’s internal behavior was consistent with the ethical claims made in their project.

This distinction between project ethics and process ethics is important. A team may produce a strong analysis of surveillance, fairness, or accountability while reproducing opacity, unequal labor, or unacknowledged authority in its own work. That contradiction is pedagogically valuable.

## 13. BBS and EDOCA Integration

BBS should remain a central analytic framework, but it should be introduced through situational awareness before moving into formal syntax. Students first need to learn to perceive blended ethical environments before they can describe them formally.

EDOCA should be integrated as a diagnostic extension, but it needs to be scaffolded carefully. Students should not receive BBS, EDOCA, mediation pathways, and ethical theory all at once. The sequence should move from observation to description to diagnosis.

The next version of the course should use a repeatable four-part diagnostic progression:

**Observe.** Students identify the ethical environment. They locate agents, institutions, stakeholders, systems, spaces, modalities, incentives, rules, and affected communities.

**Trace.** Students map the relevant Source → Vector → Destination pathways. They examine how information, authority, interpretation, harm, trust, or responsibility moves through the system.

**Diagnose.** Students apply BBS, EDOCA, stakeholder analysis, ethical theory, professional codes, or policy frameworks to locate ambiguity, asymmetry, mediation failure, structural leakage, in-system gaming, boundary confusion, or responsibility gaps.

**Revise.** Students update their ethical judgment, case analysis, design recommendation, team process, or personal/professional ethical framework.

This diagnostic progression should be used repeatedly but lightly. It should become a practical habit of ethical analysis, not a burden of terminology. BBS and EDOCA should be presented as descriptive tools rather than as prescriptive moral systems. They help students see and articulate the structure of ethical situations, but students still need to reason through value conflicts, evidence, consequences, and responsibility.

## 14. Assessment Revision

The assessment model should move toward more frequent, smaller artifacts that build toward major deliverables. The guiding principle should be that every high-stakes assignment has several low-stakes precursors.

Possible recurring artifacts include:

- Opening provocation.
- GitHub discussion post.
- Zotero evidence action.
- Journal-derived reflection.
- BBS mini-map.
- EDOCA diagnostic note.
- Peer stress-test response.
- AI-use disclosure and decision trace.
- Team process reflection.
- Final framework revision.

These should not all require heavy grading. Many can be complete/incomplete, rubric-checked, peer-reviewed, or sampled for feedback. The goal is to create rhythm, accountability, and formative assessment without overwhelming the instructor.

The assessment system should evaluate ethical growth, not only final polish. Students should be rewarded for revision, responsiveness to critique, evidence-based reasoning, awareness of complexity, and responsible documentation of human and AI contributions.

The recurring assessment rhythm should align with SRDMPA:

- Speculate through provocations and initial judgments.
- Research through Zotero evidence actions.
- Design through BBS/EDOCA maps and stakeholder analysis.
- Make/Produce through case notes, team artifacts, and framework drafts.
- Publish/Present through repository traces, peer review, and presentations.
- Assess through reflection, critique, revision, and final ethical synthesis.

Materials for the Dean should be treated as a separate dissemination track, not as the driver of the course redesign. These materials should present the course as a low-disruption contribution to Modul’s broader teaching culture.

Possible Dean-facing materials include:

- A short post-course reflection.
- A revised course architecture summary.
- A repository/wiki demonstration.
- A description of GitHub and Zotero as professional learning environments.
- A summary of the residency-anchor delivery model.
- A brief explanation of BBS and EDOCA as analytic tools.

The framing should avoid implying that other faculty must adopt these workflows. Instead, the course can be presented as a pilot that generated reusable resources and observations for interested colleagues.

The dean-facing version should also clarify that the redesign does not require changing the approved course identity. It strengthens delivery, assessment, and scaffolding while preserving the official course focus on societal and ethical impacts of data science.

## Conclusion

The next version of SEID 2364 should preserve the adaptive, experimental nature of the first offering while providing a stronger operating structure. The course should function as an ethical learning ecology: GitHub provides the shared workspace, Zotero provides the evidence infrastructure, provocations provide engagement and attendance evidence, journals support private reflection, games model ethical systems, teams create distributed responsibility, and BBS/EDOCA provide analytic language for describing mediation and ethical complexity.

The central revision is not a change in course identity. It is the stabilization of the course’s delivery architecture so that students can engage more deeply with the ethical, social, and professional implications of data science within the mediated environments they are learning to analyze.

The redesigned course should prepare students not only to evaluate present systems, but to anticipate future capability shifts; not only to comply with rules, but to reason under ambiguity; not only to analyze ethical problems, but to document, revise, and defend their own ethical practice as emerging data science professionals.
