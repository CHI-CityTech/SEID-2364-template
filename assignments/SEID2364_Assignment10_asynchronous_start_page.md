# Week 10 Asynchronous Module
## Temporal Distance, Signal Integrity, and Time-Based Mediation

This week extends our work on **spatial distance** by introducing **temporal distance**. Spatial distance is review: last week, we considered how signals move through spaces that may be personal, local, remote, or inaccessible. This week, we use that prior work as the foundation for analyzing how signals also move through time.

In BBS, a signal does not only move through space. It also moves through time.

A signal may be immediate, delayed, stored, retrieved, reinterpreted, expired, or inaccessible. These temporal conditions affect trust, signal integrity, observability, control, authority, and ethical responsibility.

Because this week is asynchronous, the structure of the class itself becomes part of the topic. We are working in a different class modality than our face-to-face sessions, and that difference should be treated as part of the analysis. You are not only studying temporal mediation; you are participating in it through a different modality. ## Core Concepts for This Week

### Spatial Distance

Spatial distance describes how accessible a space is relative to an agent and a mediation pathway.

- **Personal** — unique to one agent
- **Local** — directly accessible shared environment
- **Remote** — accessible only through mediation
- **Inaccessible** — beyond the current mediation pathway

### Temporal Distance

Temporal distance describes how accessible an event or signal is relative to time and a mediation pathway.

- **Real-Time** — operationally immediate relative to the system context
- **Latent** — delayed but predictable
- **Indeterminate / Stored** — delayed or stored for an unknown amount of time before retrieval or interpretation
- **Inaccessible / Expired** — unavailable within the relevant temporal frame, or arriving after usefulness has collapsed

### Signal Integrity

Signal integrity concerns whether the signal remains usable and trustworthy as it moves through mediation pathways.

This week, we consider both:

- **Structural integrity** — whether the signal pattern remains preserved
- **Temporal integrity** — whether the signal remains valid and useful within the relevant temporal frame

A signal may remain structurally accurate but fail temporally if it arrives too late, is stale, or is interpreted outside its proper context.
