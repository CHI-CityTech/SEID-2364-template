## Framing

Across the past several weeks, you have built a layered analytical understanding of AI-mediated systems. That work unfolded in stages:

- **Assignments 3–6** introduced you to harm identification, ethical frameworks, system structuring, and mediation pathways. By Assignment 6, you had developed a systematic method for tracing how signals, authority, and information move through a system.
- **Assignment 7** asked you to evaluate a system under the EU AI Act — to classify it, justify that classification, and identify what regulation requires and where it leaves gaps.
- **Assignment 8** introduced a different system and asked you to trace how bias enters across different deployment scenarios — showing how the same core technology produces different harms depending on context, user group, and decision authority.
- **Assignment 9** asked you to identify a real-world incident and reconstruct how harm emerged through mediation pathways — applying EDOCA to evaluate whether capability matched responsibility.

This week, you bring that work together.

Your task is not to repeat or extend any single analysis. Your task is to **synthesize** — to identify what you now understand about a system, an ethical challenge, or a class of problems when you look across everything you have done.

Because Assignments 7, 8, and 9 may not all use the same system, you have a choice:

> **Select one system, incident, or case study from your A7–A9 work that you can analyze from at least two of the three analytical perspectives (regulatory, bias, responsibility).** That becomes your synthesis focus.

This flexibility is intentional. What matters is that you are integrating multiple lenses on a real system — not that you have done all three assignments on identical material.

This is a transition week. You are moving from discovery to communication. You are learning what your analysis means and how to explain it to others.

## Part 1 — Integrative System Analysis

**Goal:** Synthesize your three prior analytical perspectives into one complete understanding of your system, its risks, harms, and responsibility structures.

### Activity 1: Choose Your Focus System

Select **one system, incident, or case study** from your work in Assignments 7, 8, or 9 that you can examine from **at least two** of the following analytical perspectives:

- Regulatory (from A7: EU AI Act classification, obligations, gaps)
- Bias and harm pathways (from A8: deployment context, design choices, harm distribution)
- Responsibility and capability (from A9: EDOCA analysis, mediation pathways, accountability structures)

You do not need to have analyzed the identical system in all three assignments. What matters is that you can bring at least two lenses to bear on a coherent subject. If your A7 and A9 systems are the same but your A8 system is different, you may choose either as your synthesis focus, or find a meaningful bridge between them.

If you are uncertain which to choose, pick the system about which you have the most to say — the one where you found the most interesting tensions or surprises in your prior analysis.

### Activity 2: Create an Integrated EDOCA + Risk + Responsibility Map

Create a **comprehensive visual or narrative document** that integrates all three analytical lenses:

**Layer 1 — Regulatory (from A7):**
- Risk classification under the EU AI Act
- What regulatory obligations apply?
- What ambiguities does the regulation leave unresolved?

**Layer 2 — Bias Pathways and Harm (from A8):**
- How does bias enter the system?
- Across your three deployment scenarios from A8, where do harms accumulate?
- What design choices enable or constrain harm?

**Layer 3 — Responsibility and Capability (from A9):**
- Using EDOCA: where is Effort concentrated? Where is Distortion highest? Who has Observability, Control, Authority?
- Where does capability diverge from stated responsibility?
- How is accountability distributed (or obscured) across the system?

**Integration Question:**
When all three lenses are applied simultaneously, what is the **core ethical tension** in this system?

Examples of core tensions:
- Regulation covers classification but not deployment context
- Bias is foreseeable but responsibility is distributed
- Control and authority are separated
- Capability exists but is hidden
- Rules conflict with incentive structures

### Deliverable for Part 1

Create one artifact that shows integration. Choose the format that best fits your analysis:

**Option A — Narrative Integration (3–5 pages)**
Write a unified system description that weaves together all three perspectives. Use subheadings to keep the three lenses distinct, but show how they inform each other.

Structure:
1. System overview (1 paragraph)
2. Regulatory analysis + gaps (1 page)
3. Bias pathways + harms (1 page)
4. EDOCA responsibility analysis (1 page)
5. Core ethical tension (1 page)
6. Visual diagram (simple diagram or table, embedded)

**Option B — Visual Integration (diagram + 1-page narrative)**
Create a structured visual (BBS diagram, risk matrix, responsibility flowchart, or custom visualization) that shows all three layers simultaneously.

Then write a 1-page narrative explaining:
- What the diagram shows
- How the three lenses interact
- What the core ethical tension is

**Option C — EDOCA Matrix with Regulatory + Bias Overlay**
Create a detailed EDOCA matrix (from A9) and add two additional columns:
- Regulatory gaps or conflicts (from A7)
- Harm vectors or bias risks (from A8)

Then write a 1-page synthesis explaining the integrated findings.

**Regardless of format, your deliverable must answer this question:**

> **What is the core ethical challenge in this system when you consider regulation, bias, responsibility, and capability together? What does the system allow to happen that frameworks alone do not address?**

## Part 2 — Paper Framework and Research Organization

**Goal:** Translate your integrated analysis into the foundation for a written paper. Define your argument, identify your research question, and begin organizing sources.

### Activity 1: Develop Your Core Research Question

Based on your integrated analysis, write a **clear, specific research question** that your final paper will answer.

Your research question should:
- Emerge directly from your system analysis (A7–A9)
- Go beyond pure description ("How does this system work?") toward interpretation ("What does this reveal about ethical judgment?")
- Be answerable through your case study + external research
- Point toward implications beyond your specific system

Examples:
- "When regulation classifies a system as 'high-risk' but deployment context makes harm foreseeable but not prosecutable, what does ethical responsibility mean?"
- "How can we design systems where control and authority are not separated in ways that obscure accountability?"
- "What additional safeguards are needed when bias is foreseeable but distributed across multiple design choices?"
- "How should we evaluate mediation systems when regulation focuses on input/output but harms emerge from latency and observability gaps?"

### Activity 2: Draft Your Paper Outline and Opening

Write a **5–8 page outline** of your planned paper, including:

**1. Tentative Title**
Create a working title that frames your argument.

**2. Opening (Introduction)**
Write or outline the first 300–500 words of your paper. This should:
- Introduce your specific system (brief context)
- Explain why it matters ethically
- State your research question clearly
- Preview your argument structure

**3. Body Section Outline**
Outline 3–4 major sections that will follow your introduction. Each section should:
- Have a clear heading
- State what argument it makes
- Identify the key evidence or analysis you will use
- Note which sources you will need (annotate with "from A7," "from A8," "need to find")

**4. Anticipated Counterarguments or Complications**
Identify where someone might disagree with you. What would they say? How will you address it?

**5. Conclusion Outline**
What will your paper argue by the end? What implications or questions will remain open?

### Activity 3: Identify and Organize Sources

Create an **annotated list of sources** you will use in your paper.

Your list should include:

**A. Sources You've Already Found (from A7–A10)**
Review your Zotero library for sources you added in Assignments 7, 8, 9, and 10.

For each source, note:
- Title and author
- Zotero link
- How you plan to use it (which section?)
- Key quote or finding

**B. New Sources You Need to Find**
Identify 3–5 additional sources that would strengthen your argument:
- Academic papers on your system or related ethical issues
- Policy documents or regulatory guidance
- Technical documentation or case studies
- News coverage or incident analysis
- Comparative analysis of similar systems

For each needed source, write:
- What question does it answer?
- Where would you search (Google Scholar, JSTOR, policy databases)?
- What keywords would help?

**C. Source Organization**
Organize your sources by how you plan to use them:
- **Foundation sources** — provide background or framing
- **Evidence sources** — provide specific data, findings, or analysis
- **Counterargument sources** — represent alternative viewpoints
- **Implications sources** — help discuss what this means beyond the case study

### Deliverable for Part 2

Submit:
1. **Research Question** (1–2 sentences)
2. **Paper Outline and Opening** (5–8 pages)
3. **Annotated Source List** (at least 10 sources total: some from A7–A10, plus 3–5 new sources you plan to find)

## Part 3 — Presentation Planning and Structure

**Goal:** Begin planning how you will communicate your analysis orally. Think about audience, time, visuals, and key takeaways.

### Activity 1: Define Your Presentation Scope and Audience

Answer these questions:

1. **Who is your audience?**
   - Peers in the class?
   - Instructor?
   - A broader stakeholder (e.g., policy maker, engineer, ethicist)?
   - The general public?
   
2. **How long will your presentation be?**
   - 5 minutes?
   - 10 minutes?
   - 15 minutes?
   - (We will clarify this in class; for now, assume 5–10 minutes unless instructed otherwise)

3. **What is your primary goal?**
   - Teach the audience about your system?
   - Convince them of a particular ethical concern?
   - Invite them to think differently about a familiar problem?
   - Propose a solution or design change?

### Activity 2: Outline Your Presentation Structure

Create a **presentation outline** with these sections:

**Opening (1–2 minutes)**
- Hook: Why should the audience care?
- Context: What system are you analyzing?
- Question: What are you asking?

**Body (3–5 minutes)**
- Main argument (what did you discover?)
- Key evidence (1–2 strongest findings from your A7–A9 analysis)
- Complication or tension (what makes this hard to solve?)

**Closing (1–2 minutes)**
- What does this mean?
- What's the implication or next step?
- What question are you leaving the audience with?

### Activity 3: Plan Visual Elements

Identify what visuals would help your presentation:

**Options:**
- A diagram showing your system (use your A5 or A6 work as a starting point)
- A chart or matrix showing your EDOCA analysis
- A timeline showing how bias enters the system (from A8)
- A regulatory compliance checklist (from A7)
- A photo or screenshot of the actual system
- A short video clip showing the system in action
- A infographic or summary graphic

For each visual, note:
- What does it show?
- When in your presentation would you show it?
- What is the single most important point it illustrates?

**Note:** You don't need to create finished visuals yet. Just plan what would be helpful.

### Deliverable for Part 3

Submit:
1. **Presentation Outline** (1–2 pages, structured by time allocation)
2. **Audience and Scope Definition** (1 paragraph)
3. **Visual Elements Checklist** (list of 3–4 planned visuals with notes on purpose and timing)
