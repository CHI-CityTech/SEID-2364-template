## Framing

Throughout this course, you have developed a set of analytical tools:

- Ethical frameworks (consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics, and others)
- BBS mediation pathway analysis (SVD, spatial and temporal distance, signal integrity)
- Regulatory evaluation (EU AI Act classification, obligations, gaps)
- Bias and harm tracing (deployment context, design choices, harm distribution)
- EDOCA capability and responsibility analysis

These tools work well when a system is known, documented, and sufficiently understood. But what happens when the technology is **new**, **poorly understood**, or **not yet regulated**?

This is the central question of Unit 11.

Emerging technologies confront ethical reasoning with a structural problem: **the analytical tools were designed for systems we understand, but the systems most in need of ethical attention are often the ones we understand least**. Regulation lags behind deployment. Harm patterns are not yet visible. Responsible parties are unclear. Long-term effects are unknown.

This does not mean ethical analysis is impossible. It means we need a different kind of reasoning — one that works under uncertainty rather than waiting for certainty.

## Core Concepts for This Unit

### Ethical Uncertainty

Ethical uncertainty is not ignorance. It is the condition of needing to act or evaluate before full information is available. Key features:

- **Epistemic uncertainty** — we do not yet know how the system behaves at scale
- **Normative uncertainty** — we do not yet agree what standard should apply
- **Temporal uncertainty** — harms may emerge slowly, long after deployment
- **Jurisdictional uncertainty** — regulation may not exist, or may be fragmented across borders

### Analogical Reasoning

When a technology is new, we reason by analogy to related technologies we understand better.

Examples:
- Facial recognition → analogous to fingerprint databases (accuracy, consent, scope creep)
- Synthetic media (deepfakes) → analogous to photo manipulation (authenticity, context, audience)
- Neural interfaces → analogous to pharmaceutical interventions (reversibility, consent, side effects)

Good analogical reasoning requires:
1. Identifying the most relevant predecessor
2. Mapping structural similarities
3. Identifying where the analogy breaks down
4. Adjusting the analysis for what is genuinely new

### The Precautionary Principle

The precautionary principle holds that **when a technology poses plausible risk of serious harm, the burden of proof lies with those deploying it — not with those who might be harmed**.

It does not require certainty of harm. It requires:
- A plausible mechanism of harm
- Stakes high enough to warrant caution
- A decision-maker with the capacity to act

Different versions of the precautionary principle set different thresholds. Some require only possibility; others require probability. Some apply only to irreversible harms; others apply to any significant harm.

### Reversibility

A key ethical question for emerging technologies: **can we undo this?**

If a system:
- modifies biological states (neural interfaces, genetic tools)
- changes social norms (surveillance normalization, behavioral prediction)
- alters permanent records (biometric databases, criminal justice scoring)
- creates dependencies (AI-mediated decision systems embedded in infrastructure)

…then harm may persist even after the system is shut down. Irreversibility changes the ethical calculus.

### Regulatory Gaps

The EU AI Act and similar frameworks were written about systems that existed at the time of drafting. Emerging technologies may:
- Not fit any existing risk category
- Create new categories of harm not anticipated by regulators
- Be governed by multiple conflicting frameworks
- Fall entirely outside existing jurisdiction

Identifying a regulatory gap does not mean no ethical obligations exist. It means those obligations must be reasoned from principles rather than rules.

## Part 1 — Emerging Technology Analysis

**Goal:** Apply your analytical tools speculatively to a technology at the frontier of current regulation.

### Step 1: Select a Scenario

Choose **one** of the following emerging technology scenarios. All are real and in active development or early deployment:

**Scenario A — Affect Recognition**
AI systems that claim to infer emotional states, engagement, or deception from facial expressions, voice patterns, or biometric signals. Currently used in hiring, education monitoring, and law enforcement.

**Scenario B — Synthetic Personhood**
AI-generated personas that interact with users over time, building ongoing relationships — customer service agents, therapeutic companions, tutors, social companions. Some disclose their nature; many do not.

**Scenario C — Predictive Policing and Risk Scoring**
Systems that predict the probability of future criminal behavior to inform bail, sentencing, parole, or patrol deployment. Used across multiple jurisdictions with limited standardization or transparency.

**Scenario D — Neurological Interface Devices**
Devices that read and/or write electrical signals to and from the brain. Currently at early clinical stages; commercial development is accelerating. Some applications are therapeutic; others are cognitive augmentation.

**Scenario E — Cultural Heritage and AI Reproduction**
AI systems trained on or used to reproduce cultural artifacts, oral traditions, artistic styles, or community-held knowledge without the consent, participation, or benefit of the originating communities.

**Scenario F — Bring Your Own**
With instructor approval, you may propose an alternative emerging technology scenario not on this list. It must: be real (not speculative fiction), be in active development or deployment, and raise genuine ethical uncertainty that current regulation does not address.

### Step 4: Apply Analogical Reasoning

Identify **one predecessor technology** that is meaningfully similar to your selected emerging technology.

Explain:
1. What is structurally similar between the two?
2. What harms or risks appeared in the predecessor that might recur here?
3. Where does the analogy break down — what is genuinely new?
4. What can we learn from how the predecessor was (or was not) regulated?

---

### Step 5: Speculative BBS and EDOCA Analysis

Using BBS and EDOCA as speculative tools, anticipate (not describe) how this technology would function as a mediation pathway.

For at least **two plausible SVD pathways**:
- **Source → Vector → Destination**: what signal originates where, passes through what mediation, and arrives where?
- **EDOCA at the critical transition**: where will Effort be concentrated? Where is Distortion likely to occur? Who will have Observability? Who will have Control? Who will have Authority?

You are not describing what currently happens — you are anticipating what is likely to happen given the system's structure. Mark your analysis as speculative throughout.

### Step 6: Regulatory Gap Analysis

Answer:
1. Does the EU AI Act apply to this technology in its current form? If so, under what classification?
2. Where does regulation leave gaps — what harms could occur that current frameworks do not address?
3. If you were advising a regulator, what single provision would you add to address the most critical gap?

### Step 7: Ethical Evaluation Under Uncertainty

Apply the precautionary principle to your scenario:
1. What is the plausible mechanism of harm?
2. How serious and how reversible is that harm?
3. Who bears the risk? Who decides?
4. What threshold of evidence should trigger regulatory action?
5. What would a defensible minimum standard look like for this technology right now, even before formal regulation?

## Part 2 — Reflection: Your Own Threshold for Action

In 300–500 words, reflect on the following:

Throughout this assignment, you have been reasoning about a technology where certainty is not available. Based on that experience, address:

1. **What is your personal threshold for raising an ethical concern?**
   Do you require evidence of harm, or does plausible possibility of harm suffice?

2. **How does uncertainty change your ethical reasoning?**
   Does not knowing make you more cautious, or does it reduce the urgency of action?

3. **Where do you locate responsibility in conditions of uncertainty?**
   With the developer? The regulator? The deployer? The user? Someone else?

4. **What would change your mind?**
   If you concluded that the technology you analyzed was too risky to deploy now — what evidence would you need to see before reconsidering?

There is no single correct answer. The goal is to make your reasoning visible and to take a position.
