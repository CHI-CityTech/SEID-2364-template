# SEID 2364 – Final Assignment
## Ethical Position Statements Portfolio

### Purpose

This assignment is the culminating assessment for SEID 2364 and replaces a traditional final exam.

The purpose of this course has not been to provide a fixed set of ethical answers or to require memorization of existing standards and frameworks. Instead, it has been to develop your ability to **analyze ethical situations with sensitivity to complexity, impact, and responsibility** across systems, stakeholders, and contexts.

Because ethical standards vary across societal institutions, governmental authorities, and specific project conditions, there is no single "correct" answer. Ethical judgments depend upon the evaluation framework being used and the perspectives of the stakeholders involved.

You are therefore asked to **state your current ethical positions**—personally and professionally—and **analyze how those positions are informed by course concepts, frameworks, and professional responsibilities**. Your task is to demonstrate synthesis: how reflection, theory, systems thinking, professional standards, and governance shape what you now believe and how you would act.

In this assignment you will:
- reflect on how your thinking developed;
- articulate a **Personal Ethical Position Statement** and a **Professional Ethical Position Statement**;
- analyze how these positions align with philosophical ideas, professional standards, and governmental authority;
- evaluate how **robust and adaptable** your positions are in light of future technological and societal change (Big Idea 5).
- include an **AI Use Statement** with a brief BBS mediation pathway (Source-Vector-Destination);
- ground your analysis in course and external sources documented through your course Zotero workflow.

The emphasis is on **clarity of position, quality of reasoning, and integration of course material**, not on agreement with any particular viewpoint.

# Section 2 – Personal Ethical Position Statement

Sections 2 and 3 each have two parts and should be organized as separate sections.

Section 2 focuses on your ethical identity: what you believe, why you believe it, and why you can stand behind it.

## A. Personal Ethical Position Statement

Develop a **concise, powerful single paragraph** that states your current personal ethical position.

This paragraph should articulate the principles, values, commitments, priorities, and boundaries that guide your ethical decision-making as an individual, and indicate how those values guide action under conditions of conflict or complexity.

It is important to distinguish between a **description** and a **position**.

A description states general beliefs without commitment or application:

- "I believe fairness is important."
- "Transparency matters in ethical decision-making."

A position makes a claim about how those values guide action:

- "I prioritize fairness over efficiency when system outcomes disproportionately impact vulnerable populations."
- "I support transparency in data systems unless disclosure introduces significant and preventable harm to individuals or groups."

Your goal is to produce a **clear, declarative paragraph** that someone could use to understand how you would reason and act.

Importantly, values such as "fairness," "transparency," or "efficiency" are not fixed or universally defined; their meaning and application can change depending on context, stakeholders, and systems. A strong position statement therefore does more than list values—it **prioritizes them**. You should make clear which values take precedence when they come into conflict, and how you make those determinations across different types of harm, belief systems, and impacts.

You may wish to consider:

- What values are most important to you?
- What principles guide your decisions?
- Which values take precedence when values conflict?
- What ethical responsibilities do you believe all individuals possess?
- What ethical boundaries are important to maintain?

## B. Analysis and Influences

After articulating your position, explain and defend it.

Show not only how you arrived at this position, but why you judge it to be ethically justified, practically defensible, and worth standing behind.

This analysis should draw explicitly on course materials and experiences.

For example:

- Do elements of your position align with consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics, care ethics, or other ethical traditions?
- Were your views influenced by particular discussions, activities, case studies, or journal reflections?
- Which course concepts (e.g., Big Ideas, BBS, EDOCA) appear most consistent with your position?

The objective is not to force your position into a particular philosophical framework. Rather, it is to identify and analyze relationships between your position and ideas encountered during the course, and to make a clear argument for why your position is one you can responsibly stand behind.

# Section 3 – Professional Ethical Position Statement

Section 3 focuses on your professional obligation: what you owe as a data scientist, why you owe it, and why that duty is defensible in practice.

## A. Professional Ethical Position Statement

Develop a **concise, powerful single paragraph** that states your current professional ethical position as a data scientist.

This paragraph should clearly articulate the ethical responsibilities you recognize in the design, development, deployment, and management of data-driven systems, including how you would act when responsibilities, stakeholders, or constraints are in tension.

Your statement should move beyond general values and indicate **how professional responsibilities guide action in practice**.

Importantly, professional values such as "fairness," "transparency," "privacy," or "efficiency" are not fixed or universally defined; their meaning and application vary across systems, stakeholders, and institutional contexts. A strong professional position therefore does more than list responsibilities—it **prioritizes them**. You should make clear which responsibilities take precedence when they come into conflict (e.g., transparency vs. harm prevention, efficiency vs. fairness), and how you make those determinations in real system design and data management contexts.

You may wish to consider:

- What responsibilities do data scientists have toward users, stakeholders, organizations, and society?
- What role do transparency, accountability, fairness, privacy, and stewardship play in professional practice?
- What responsibilities remain even when they are inconvenient or costly?
- What ethical responsibilities cannot be delegated?

## B. Analysis: Professional Standards and Influences

After articulating your position, explain and defend it.

Show not only how your professional position relates to professional expectations, standards, and institutional contexts, but why you judge it to be ethically justified, professionally defensible, and worth standing behind in practice.

You may draw on:

- ACM Code of Ethics
- IEEE Code of Ethics
- Other relevant professional standards in your field
- Organizational policies or norms
- Prior assignments, case studies, and course discussions

ACM and IEEE are examples explored in class; you may also incorporate other professional frameworks relevant to your discipline or interests.

The goal is to identify areas of alignment, tension, agreement, or disagreement between your professional position and established professional expectations, and to justify why your professional position remains ethically and professionally defensible.

# Section 5 – Position Resilience and Future Capabilities (Big Idea 5)

One of the recurring themes of this course is that technological and societal capabilities often evolve more rapidly than ethical frameworks, professional standards, and regulatory structures.

This section has three required parts.

## A. Future Scenario (Plausible, Not Fantastical)

Construct a **plausible future scenario** in which your current ethical positions may not apply cleanly or may come under stress.

Your scenario should be:

- Grounded in course-relevant domains (e.g., AI/ML systems, data ecosystems, platforms, governance, automation, decision systems)
- A reasonable extension of current trends (e.g., advanced AI systems, large-scale data integration, autonomous decision-making, emerging quantum applications where relevant)
- Situated within a recognizable institutional or societal context (e.g., healthcare, finance, education, public policy, industry)

Avoid purely fantastical scenarios (e.g., supernatural events, arbitrary rule changes). You are encouraged to be imaginative, but your scenario should remain grounded enough that meaningful ethical analysis is possible. The goal is not speculation for its own sake, but **applied ethical stress-testing** under conditions that could plausibly emerge or be meaningfully reasoned about.

## B. Stress Test of Your Positions

Using your scenario, evaluate your Personal and Professional Ethical Position Statements.

Consider:

- Which aspects of your positions remain stable?
- Which aspects become difficult to apply?
- Where do tensions emerge (e.g., between values, stakeholders, or responsibilities)?
- Do personal and professional positions come into conflict in this scenario?

Your analysis should demonstrate how your positions function under pressure, not just how they appear in ideal conditions.

## C. Adaptation and Revision

Based on your analysis, reflect on how your positions may need to adapt.

Consider:

- What would you revise, refine, or clarify?
- Which assumptions in your positions depend on current technologies, norms, or regulations?
- How confident are you that your positions can respond effectively to significant technological or societal change?

This section is not intended to predict the future. Rather, it is intended to evaluate the **adaptability and robustness** of your current ethical positions through application in a plausible scenario.

# Section 6 – AI Use Statement

Students are required to include an **AI Use Statement** describing how artificial intelligence tools were used throughout the course and in the preparation of this assignment.

This statement should be **transparent, specific, and reflective**, and should address both **tool use** and **collaborative use** of AI.

**Exemplar:** Review the [AI Usage Statement for SEID 2364](../seid-2364_ai_collaboration_report.md#ai-usage-statement--seid-2364-course-development-spring-2026) to see how the instructor documented AI use across the semester, including the BBS mediation pathway framework. This is a worked example of the kind of reflection and transparency expected in your statement.

You should consider:

- Which AI tools you used (e.g., ChatGPT, Copilot, other systems)
- How you used them (e.g., drafting, editing, brainstorming, analysis, formatting, summarization)
- Whether the AI functioned primarily as a **tool** (single-step output) or a **collaborative partner** (iterative interaction)
- How AI use influenced your thinking, writing, or decision-making
- Any limitations, concerns, or ethical considerations you observed in your use of AI

## Mediation Pathway (BBS) Requirement

Include a brief **mediation pathway description** of your AI use, using the course’s BBS framing (Source–Vector–Destination). You may present this as short prose, a simple diagram, or structured notation.

At minimum, identify:

- **Source(s)**: who/what initiated the signal (e.g., you, prior drafts, datasets)
- **Vector(s)**: the AI systems and transformations applied (e.g., prompts, iterations, edits)
- **Destination(s)**: where the output was received or acted upon (e.g., your draft, decisions, revisions)

You may also note transformations across spaces (conceptual/virtual/physical), iterations, and feedback loops. The goal is to make the **pathway of collaboration visible**, not to provide a perfect formal model.

This section is not evaluated based on how much or how little AI you used. It is evaluated on the **clarity and honesty of your reflection** and your ability to represent and reason about AI mediation within your workflow.

Where appropriate, you may connect your AI use to course concepts such as:

- Collaboration vs. tool use
- Mediation pathways (BBS)
- Responsibility and authorship
- Trust, bias, and system behavior

The purpose of this section is to make your process visible and to encourage critical reflection on AI as part of your ethical and professional practice.
