## Framing

In Assignment 12, you articulated a personal ethical framework. You identified your core principles, located their limits, and considered what would change your mind.

Assignment 13 puts that framework under pressure.

A personal ethical framework is only as useful as it is robust. It needs to work not just in familiar territory — the systems you have already analyzed, the harms you already understand, the populations you already see — but in contexts that are genuinely different from your prior experience.

This assignment places your framework in three kinds of novel contexts:

1. **A different domain** — a system in a field you have not analyzed before
2. **A different cultural and jurisdictional frame** — where your framework may not apply straightforwardly
3. **A genuinely ambiguous case** — where there is no clearly correct answer and your framework produces tension

The goal is not to prove your framework right. The goal is to discover what it does and does not handle well — and to refine it accordingly.

## Learning Goals

By the end of this assignment, you should be able to:

1. Apply your personal ethical framework to systems outside your primary domain of analysis
2. Recognize where cultural context, jurisdictional difference, and local values change the ethical calculus
3. Identify cases where your framework produces genuine tension or contradictory guidance
4. Articulate the difference between a difficult case (where the answer is hard) and an ambiguous case (where multiple answers are defensible)
5. Refine your framework in response to what you discover, with specific revisions rather than vague acknowledgment

## Part 2 — Cross-Cultural and Cross-Jurisdictional Application

**Goal:** Examine how your framework performs when cultural context and jurisdictional norms differ from those you assumed in developing it.

Most frameworks developed in Western academic and regulatory contexts carry embedded assumptions: individualism, formal rights, legalistic accountability, market-based incentive structures, and a specific conception of privacy as individual control.

These assumptions are not universal. In many contexts, ethics is understood through community obligations, relational identity, collective harm, elder authority, or cosmological responsibility — frameworks that do not map cleanly onto Western regulatory logic.

### Step 1: Select a Case

Choose **one** of the following cross-cultural scenarios, or propose your own with instructor approval:

**Case A — Community Knowledge and AI Training**
An AI company trains a language model on oral literature, traditional medicine knowledge, and ceremonial texts from indigenous communities without their consent or participation. The company argues the data was publicly accessible. The communities argue the knowledge belongs to the community, not to any individual, and cannot be consented to by any single member.

**Case B — Facial Recognition in a Low-Income Country**
A government in a lower-income country deploys AI-based facial recognition for social benefit distribution. The stated goal is to reduce fraud and ensure aid reaches intended recipients. Critics argue the system replicates error patterns from training data built on higher-income populations, and that "fraud prevention" is being used to justify surveillance of poverty.

**Case C — AI-Mediated Hiring in a Collectivist Cultural Context**
A multinational company implements a global AI hiring system. In markets where individualist résumé-and-interview logic is the norm, the system performs adequately. In markets where relationships, community networks, and family reputation are central to professional identity, the system systematically screens out culturally appropriate candidates.

**Case D — Predictive Health AI in Contexts of Medical Distrust**
An AI health-screening system is deployed in a community with a documented history of medical exploitation. The system performs well by clinical accuracy metrics. Residents refuse to use it. Public health officials consider making participation mandatory.

### Step 2: Analyze Using Your Framework (400–600 words)

Apply your personal ethical framework to your selected case:

1. What are the key ethical tensions?
2. Which of your principles apply? Which do not apply straightforwardly?
3. Where does your framework produce guidance that feels culturally specific rather than universal?
4. Whose perspective is your framework best equipped to represent? Whose is it least equipped to represent?

### Step 3: Revise or Extend (200–300 words)

Based on this analysis, propose one specific revision or extension to your personal ethical framework that would help it function better across cultural and jurisdictional differences.

Be specific: what would you add, remove, or qualify? Why?

## Part 3 — The Genuinely Ambiguous Case

**Goal:** Encounter a case where your framework gives you not a clear answer but a genuine dilemma — and practice reasoning through it honestly.

### What Makes a Case Genuinely Ambiguous?

A hard case is difficult because the right answer requires painful tradeoffs. You know what you should do, but doing it costs something real.

A genuinely ambiguous case is different. In an ambiguous case, your ethical framework produces two or more defensible conclusions simultaneously. You are not choosing between good and bad — you are choosing between two goods in tension, or two harms you cannot both avoid.

### Step 1: Identify Your Own Ambiguous Case

From your work across A1–A12, identify **one case, decision, or system** that you found genuinely ambiguous — where your framework did not give you a clear answer and where multiple positions seemed defensible.

This could be:
- A scenario from a prior assignment where you were uncertain
- A case from the news or your field
- A hypothetical generated by pushing your framework to its edge

Describe the case (150–250 words): what is at stake, who is affected, what makes it ambiguous.

### Step 2: Map the Tension (200–300 words)

Identify the two (or more) defensible positions your framework generates:

- What does Position A say? What principle does it follow?
- What does Position B say? What principle does it follow?
- Why can't you simply apply one principle and set the other aside?

### Step 3: Take a Position (300–400 words)

Despite the genuine ambiguity, take a position. Explain:

1. Which conclusion you find most defensible, and why
2. What you are giving up by taking that position
3. What conditions would cause you to switch to the other position
4. What this case reveals about your framework — is this a gap, a known limitation, or an irresolvable tension?

Taking a position does not require certainty. It requires intellectual honesty about where you stand and why.
