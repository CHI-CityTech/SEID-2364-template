## Part 1 — Final Paper

### Overview

Write a **final paper** of approximately **3,000–4,500 words** (excluding references and appendices) that makes and defends an ethical argument about a real AI-mediated system.

The paper is not a description of a system. It is an **argument** — you take a position, support it with evidence and analysis, acknowledge complications, and reach a conclusion.

---

### Paper Requirements

**1. System and Scope**

Your paper must focus on a **real, specific AI-mediated system or class of systems**. You may use:

- Your primary case study from A7–A10
- A system from A11 (emerging technologies)
- A system you analyzed in A12 or A13
- A new system, with instructor approval

The system must be specific enough to analyze concretely. "AI in healthcare" is not specific enough. "Algorithmic triage tools in emergency department resource allocation" is.

**2. Research Question**

Your paper must be structured around one clear research question. State it explicitly in your introduction.

The research question should:
- Be answerable through analysis and evidence (not purely speculative)
- Point toward implications beyond your specific system
- Emerge from the tensions you discovered in your prior analysis

**3. Analytical Depth**

Your argument must demonstrate command of at least **three** of the following analytical tools:

- **BBS mediation pathway analysis** — trace how signals, information, authority, or agency move through the system
- **EDOCA** — evaluate Effort, Distortion, Observability, Control, and Authority at critical transitions
- **EU AI Act or regulatory analysis** — what does current regulation require, allow, or fail to address?
- **Bias pathway analysis** — how does bias enter, travel, and produce harm across deployment contexts?
- **Personal ethical framework** — apply your A12 framework to the system and defend your conclusions

You do not need to use all five. Use the three (or more) that illuminate your argument most effectively.

**4. Evidence and Sources**

Your paper must include at least **10 sources**, organized to support your argument:

- At least 3 from your Zotero library (accumulated across A7–A13)
- At least 3 academic sources (peer-reviewed papers, reports from research institutions)
- At least 1 policy or regulatory source (EU AI Act, national policy, institutional guidelines)
- At least 1 critical or counterargument source (a source that complicates or challenges your position)

All sources must be cited consistently (APA, MLA, Chicago, or an alternative approved by the instructor).

**5. Personal Ethical Framework**

Your paper must include a section (or integrate throughout) that makes explicit how your personal ethical framework from A12 grounds your argument.

This does not need to be a long section. It should be clear to the reader what ethical principles are guiding your analysis and why.

**6. Limitations and Honest Uncertainty**

Your paper must acknowledge where your analysis is limited, where evidence is incomplete, or where your framework produces tension rather than resolution.

This is not a weakness — it demonstrates intellectual honesty and rigor.

---

### Paper Structure (Recommended)

You may organize your paper differently if it serves your argument. The following structure is a strong default:

**Introduction (300–500 words)**
- Introduce the system and why it matters
- State your research question
- Preview your argument and paper structure

**Background and Context (400–600 words)**
- Describe the system specifically
- Explain its deployment context, stakeholders, and current use
- Identify the ethical concerns motivating your analysis

**Analytical Framework (300–500 words)**
- Explain which analytical tools you are using and why
- State your personal ethical framework (briefly)
- Explain how they apply to this system

**Analysis (1,200–2,000 words)**
- Apply your chosen analytical tools to the system
- Present your findings with evidence
- Build toward your central claim

**Core Argument (400–600 words)**
- State your position clearly
- Support it with evidence from your analysis
- Acknowledge the strongest counterargument and respond to it

**Implications and Recommendations (300–400 words)**
- What does your analysis imply beyond this specific system?
- What should be done? By whom?
- What remains unresolved or requires further analysis?

**Conclusion (200–300 words)**
- Restate your argument
- Reflect on what your analysis reveals about ethical reasoning in data-driven systems
- End with one question your analysis could not answer

**References**
- Full citation list

## Part 2 — Oral Presentation

### Overview

Deliver a **10-minute oral presentation** to the class during the Week 14 face-to-face residency, followed by 5 minutes of questions.

Your presentation is not a reading of your paper. It is a **communication event** — you are making your argument to an audience in real time.

---

### Presentation Requirements

**1. Argument First**

Your presentation should lead with your argument, not your background. The audience can read background; they are here to hear your thinking.

Open with your claim. Then build toward it.

**2. Time Allocation (10 minutes)**

| Segment | Time | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | 1–2 min | Your claim and why it matters |
| System context | 1–2 min | What the system is; who is affected |
| Core analysis | 4–5 min | Your 2–3 most important analytical findings |
| Implications | 1 min | What follows from your analysis |
| Closing | 30 sec | One question your analysis leaves open |

**3. Visuals**

Include at least **2 visual elements** that support your argument. Options:

- A BBS mediation diagram of your system
- An EDOCA matrix or table showing critical transitions
- A regulatory risk classification chart
- A bias pathway diagram or scenario comparison
- A chart or data visualization
- An image or screenshot of the actual system

Visuals should be simple and argument-driven. Each visual should communicate one clear point. Do not use visuals as decoration.

**4. Audience Awareness**

Your audience is your classmates and instructor. They are familiar with BBS, EDOCA, the EU AI Act, and your general topic area — but they have not read your paper. Do not assume they know what your system does or why you chose it.

**5. Questions**

After your presentation, there will be 5 minutes of questions. Questions may come from classmates, the instructor, or both.

You are not expected to have all the answers. You are expected to respond thoughtfully, acknowledge limitations honestly, and engage with challenges rather than deflect them.

---

### Preparation

**At least one week before:**
- Your argument should be finalized
- Outline and visual plan should be complete

**Three days before:**
- Practice your presentation at least once in full
- Time yourself
- Ensure visuals are clear and loading correctly

**Day of presentation:**
- Arrive with your materials ready
- You may use notes, but aim to present — not read
- If using slides, email or share a backup copy in advance

## A Note on This Course

This course was built around a central conviction: that ethical reasoning is not a set of rules to be memorized and applied, but a practice — a way of seeing systems, identifying what they do to people, asking who decides and why, and being willing to take a position and defend it.

The final paper and presentation are not a test of what you remember. They are an opportunity to demonstrate that practice.

What the best submissions have in common is not that they reach the right answer. It is that they ask a real question, look at it honestly, and say something that is genuinely theirs.

That is what we are looking for.
