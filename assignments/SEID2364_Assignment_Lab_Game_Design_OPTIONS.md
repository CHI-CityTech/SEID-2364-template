---

## Overview

The U10SG (Unit 10 Signal-Tracking Game) and all associated game materials have been **extracted from Assignment 10** to allow students to focus on system synthesis and paper preparation.

This document presents **three options** for how to position and structure the game material as a **separate, non-urgent assignment or lab activity**. 

Choose the option that best fits your course flow and student needs.

---

## Option A: Assignment 10 Lab — Optional Enrichment

**Timing:** Week 10–11, asynchronous, optional  
**Scope:** Relatively small (2–4 hours per student)  
**Positioning:** "Extend your system analysis through game design"

### Structure

Students who have completed Assignment 10 (synthesis) may optionally engage with the game lab to:

1. **Apply their A7/8/9 analysis to game rule design**
   - Choose one ethical tension from their system analysis
   - Design one game rule that illustrates or tests that tension
   - Example: If their system shows that "control and authority are separated," design a game rule where information-holder and decision-maker are different players

2. **Play-test one signal card**
   - Use the U10SG Wiki space
   - Complete 1–2 turns of the game with the starter signal cards
   - Observe how the game structure reveals (or hides) ethical issues

3. **Propose one rule change**
   - Based on their system analysis, what rule would make the game better represent the complexity they discovered?
   - Write a 1-page proposal: rule change + ethical rationale

### Deliverable
- 1-page rule design document + ethical analysis
- Optional: wiki contributions and discussion board posts

### Grading
- Completion-based
- Evaluated on depth of ethical reasoning, not game mechanics

## Option B: Assignment 11 Lab — Signal Tracking Game Design & Rule Theory

**Timing:** Week 11–12, after Assignment 10 synthesis is complete  
**Scope:** Medium (4–8 hours per student)  
**Positioning:** "Design and test mediation systems using game mechanics"

### Structure

A full assignment that treats game design as a formal method for exploring ethical mediation:

**Part 1 — Game Rule Design (2–3 hours)**
- Students use their A7/8/9 findings to design 3–5 game rules
- Each rule must illustrate a specific ethical challenge from their system analysis
- Rules must be testable, time-based, and involve at least one mediation pathway

**Part 2 — Play-Testing and Iteration (2–3 hours)**
- Groups play-test each other's rules in the Wiki
- Collect observations about what the rules reveal
- Iterate based on play-test feedback

**Part 3 — Reflection and Rule Documentation (1–2 hours)**
- Write a 2–3 page design document explaining:
  - How your rules embody ethical concepts from BBS and EDOCA
  - What happens when players interact with the rules
  - What you learned about responsibility, capability, and authority through the design process

### Deliverable
- 3–5 game rules (formatted for wiki)
- 2–3 page design rationale
- Play-test observation notes
- Revised rules based on feedback

### Grading
- Rubric: clarity, ethical depth, testability, reflection quality

### Timeline
- Submit Part 1 by [date]
- Complete Part 2 play-testing by [date]
- Submit Part 3 by [date]

### File to Create
`SEID2364_Assignment11_Lab_Game_Design_and_Rule_Theory.md`

## Option C: Post-Paper Collaborative Refinement — "Game Design for Next Year"

**Timing:** After final papers submitted (Weeks 12–14)  
**Scope:** Small to medium (2–5 hours, flexible)  
**Positioning:** "Contribute to course evolution"

### Structure

After students have completed their individual papers and presentations, they come together to **collaboratively refine the U10SG system for next year's cohort**.

**Activity 1 — Rule Synthesis (1–2 hours)**
- Review all play-test notes and rule proposals from the course
- Identify the 5–7 most effective game mechanics
- Combine them into a cohesive, playable rule set

**Activity 2 — Rule Documentation (1–2 hours)**
- Write a full game rulebook (3–5 pages)
- Include examples, terminology, and play-test findings
- Create a quick-start guide

**Activity 3 — Student Contribution (optional, 1–2 hours)**
- Students may propose one additional rule or mechanic
- Peer vote on which make it into the final rules

### Deliverable
- Finalized U10SG Rules (v2)
- Quick-Start Guide
- Annotated Design Notes (explaining why each rule is included)

### Grading
- Completion-based with peer review
- Credited as "Course Contribution" rather than individual grade

### Positioning
- "Help build the resource that will support next year's students"
- Recognition: students' names acknowledged in the rulebook
- Optional: share findings with broader course community

### File to Create
`SEID2364_Assignment_Collaborative_Game_Refinement.md` (or integrate into syllabus as Week 14 activity)

## Recommendation

**Best fit depends on your goal:**

- **If you want the game to stay connected to A7/8/9 synthesis:** Choose **Option A (Lab 10)** — students design one rule immediately after completing synthesis
- **If you want a more developed game design activity:** Choose **Option B (Lab 11)** — students spend more time on iterative design and play-testing
- **If you want to emphasize course evolution and student agency:** Choose **Option C (Post-Paper)** — students refine the game as a community project after completing individual work

**Hybrid approach:** You could use **Option A (quick lab in Week 10 for interested students)** plus **Option C (community refinement in Week 14)**, allowing students to engage at different intensities.
