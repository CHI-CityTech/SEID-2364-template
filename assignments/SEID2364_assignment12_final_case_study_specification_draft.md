## Required Sections

Use all required section headings. Some sections may be more developed than others at this stage. By next week, the goal is not to finish the final argument; the goal is to create a usable framework for continued development.

### 1. Case Selected

Identify the Lite Case Study you selected. Briefly describe the system in your own words.

Your description should be concise but specific. Do not begin with a moral judgment. First explain what the system is, what it does, and why it matters ethically.

### 2. System Goal

What does the system claim to accomplish?

This may not be the same as what every actor wants. For example, a platform may claim to protect users, improve information quality, increase engagement, reduce harm, comply with law, or support institutional trust. Your task is to identify the stated or implied goal of the system.

Also note whether the system goal appears stable, contested, or ambiguous.

### 3. Agents / Players

Identify the major agents or players in the case.

Agents may include individuals, institutions, companies, regulators, professional groups, affected communities, technical systems, or automated decision processes. If you include an AI system, algorithm, model, or recommender as an agent, explain why. If you treat it instead as a mediation pathway or system component, explain that choice.

Include at least **three** agents.

### 4. Player Goals and Victory Conditions

For at least three agents, identify:

- what the agent wants;
- what would count as success for that agent;
- whether that success aligns with or conflicts with the system goal;
- whether that success creates costs or risks for other agents.

Use the distinction between goal, victory condition, and ethical condition:

- **Goal:** what an agent wants.
- **Victory condition:** what counts as success for that agent.
- **Ethical condition:** what would count as a responsible or acceptable outcome for the system.

Your analysis should identify at least one conflict between victory conditions.

### 5. Signals and Signal Powers

Identify the major signals moving through the system.

Signals may include data, posts, rankings, scores, labels, recommendations, warnings, decisions, classifications, reports, appeals, outputs, documentation, policies, or professional judgments.

For the major signal or signals, identify who can:

- generate the signal;
- amplify the signal;
- verify the signal;
- distort the signal;
- suppress or remove the signal;
- authorize the signal;
- reinterpret the signal.

Explain why signal control matters ethically in your case.

### 6. Rules, Constraints, and Meta-Game

Describe the internal rules of the system. What actions are allowed, blocked, delayed, rewarded, punished, or made costly?

Then describe the **meta-game**: the larger environment in which the system operates. This may include law, regulation, professional codes, institutional incentives, market pressure, public opinion, funding structures, media attention, enforcement limits, reputational risk, or cross-platform behavior.

Identify at least one way the larger meta-game changes what happens inside the system.

### 7. Information and Observability

What information is visible, hidden, partially known, delayed, or distorted?

Be specific about who can see what. Do not simply say “the system is opaque.” Identify where observability differs across agents.

Consider questions such as:

- What can users or affected people see?
- What can system designers or deployers see?
- What can regulators or auditors see?
- What can the public see?
- What information exists but is inaccessible?
- What information becomes visible only after harm has occurred?

Connect this section to BBS and/or EDOCA where appropriate.

### 8. EDOCA Diagnosis

Apply at least one EDOCA dimension to your case. You may use more than one if helpful.

EDOCA dimensions:

- **Effort:** What does it cost to act responsibly, verify information, intervene, appeal, or correct harm?
- **Distortion:** Where does information change, degrade, simplify, bias, or misrepresent reality?
- **Observability:** Who can see the relevant process, signal, action, or harm?
- **Control:** Who can intervene, alter the system, change the pathway, or stop harm?
- **Authority:** Who has legitimate or formal power to decide, enforce, classify, or authorize action?

Your EDOCA analysis should identify at least one ethical problem that becomes clearer through the framework.

### 9. Ethical Instability

Identify where the system becomes ethically unstable.

An ethical instability occurs when the structure of the system allows one agent to achieve a local goal or victory condition while producing broader harm, risk, unfairness, distortion, loss of trust, or accountability failure.

For example:

- a platform increases engagement while amplifying misinformation;
- an institution improves efficiency while reducing contestability;
- a model improves aggregate accuracy while harming a subgroup;
- a company reduces cost while shifting risk onto users;
- a regulator increases compliance while reducing flexibility or context sensitivity.

Explain the instability in structural terms. Avoid reducing the issue to “bad people making bad choices.”

### 10. Evidence Plan

Identify at least three sources you will need for the final paper. These should be entered or prepared for entry in Zotero.

For each source, explain:

- what kind of source it is;
- what claim it might support;
- what perspective or evidence it provides;
- what limitation, bias, or uncertainty it may have.

Your evidence plan should include, where possible:

- one source establishing the factual system context;
- one source addressing ethical or social consequences;
- one source addressing governance, regulation, professional standards, technical structure, or institutional practice;
- one source that complicates or challenges your initial position.

You do not need all four categories if your case does not support them yet, but you should identify what is missing.

### 11. Unresolved Ethical Question

End the assignment with one unresolved ethical question that your final paper will need to investigate.

This question should not be trivial. It should identify a real uncertainty, conflict, or tradeoff in the case.

Examples:

- Who should have authority when the actor with the most control lacks public legitimacy?
- How much transparency is required before a system can be considered accountable?
- When does personalization become manipulation?
- How should responsibility be distributed when harm emerges from many small decisions rather than one clear decision point?
- What level of evidence is necessary before intervention becomes ethically justified?
