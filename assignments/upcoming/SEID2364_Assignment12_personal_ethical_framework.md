## Part 1 — Retrospective: Where You Started and Where You Are

**Goal:** Ground your statement in an honest account of your development as an ethical reasoner across this course.

### Step 1: Return to Assignment 1

Re-read your Assignment 1 journal entry — specifically the framing questions you recorded and your initial ethical position.

### Step 2: Write a Retrospective Reflection (400–600 words)

Address the following:

1. **What were your starting assumptions?**
   What did you believe about ethics, data systems, and responsibility before this course?

2. **What changed?**
   What assignment, reading, case study, or discussion most significantly changed or complicated your thinking? Be specific — name the moment.

3. **What surprised you?**
   Was there a finding, argument, or case study that you did not expect and that genuinely unsettled your prior view?

4. **What stayed the same?**
   Are there convictions you held at the start that your analysis has only strengthened? What are they, and why did they hold?

Do not write what you think you should say. Write what is actually true about how your thinking developed.

## Part 2 — Framework Articulation: Your Ethical Principles

**Goal:** State your ethical framework as precisely as possible — in your own terms, grounded in your actual reasoning.

### Step 1: Name Your Primary Framework

Which of the frameworks introduced in this course (or beyond it) best describes how you actually reason about ethics?

- Consequentialism — you evaluate actions by their outcomes and effects on wellbeing
- Deontology — you evaluate actions by whether they respect duties, rights, and rules
- Virtue ethics — you evaluate actions by whether they reflect the character of a good person
- Care ethics — you prioritize relationships, context, and the needs of particular others
- Justice and fairness — you evaluate actions by whether they are fair and equal across affected parties
- A hybrid or your own synthesis

You are not required to pick one framework. Many people reason differently depending on context. If that is true for you, describe when and why you shift.

### Step 2: State Your Core Principles (3–5 principles)

Write **3–5 statements** that represent your core ethical principles. These should be specific enough to generate concrete guidance — not generic moral platitudes.

**Not useful:** "I believe in treating everyone fairly."

**More useful:** "When a system's design concentrates risk on groups who have the least ability to contest or appeal decisions, I believe the burden of justification falls on those who built and deployed the system — not on those who are harmed."

Each principle should include:
- The principle itself (1–2 sentences)
- Why you hold it (brief grounding — where does it come from?)
- At least one place in your A1–A11 work where you saw it operating (or failing to operate)

### Step 3: Identify Your Limits (200–300 words)

Where does your framework struggle?

Every framework has cases it handles poorly. Consequentialism can justify harm to individuals for aggregate benefit. Deontology can produce rigid outcomes in complex situations. Your own hybrid framework has its own tensions.

Identify at least one kind of situation where your framework gives you unsatisfying or contradictory guidance. You do not need to resolve it. You need to acknowledge it.

## Part 3 — Application: Your Framework in a Real Scenario

**Goal:** Demonstrate that your principles are not abstract — they produce concrete guidance.

### Step 1: Choose a Scenario

Select a professional scenario from your field of study or intended career. It should involve:

- A real tension between competing values (not a case with an obvious answer)
- A decision under uncertainty (you cannot know all the consequences in advance)
- Multiple affected parties with different levels of power and visibility

You may use one of the following, or identify your own with instructor approval:

**Option A:** A data scientist at a company is asked to build a model that will be used to automate credit decisions. They discover that the training data systematically underrepresents certain demographic groups. Their manager says the model is "good enough" to deploy. What do they do?

**Option B:** A product team at a tech company is designing an AI assistant for healthcare use. The assistant will sometimes give incorrect information. The legal team wants disclaimers; the product team wants to minimize friction. Who is right, and what should you do if you are on the team?

**Option C:** A government agency uses an AI system to flag welfare fraud. The system has a high false-positive rate in low-income communities but is cheaper to operate than manual review. You are an analyst who discovers this. What are your obligations?

**Option D:** A researcher publishes a tool that could be used both to detect and to generate synthetic media. The researcher argues openness supports accountability; critics argue it enables harm. If you were the researcher, what would you do?

### Step 2: Apply Your Framework (400–600 words)

Walk through the scenario using your stated principles:

1. Who is affected, and how?
2. What does your framework say about what should be done?
3. Are any of your principles in tension with each other in this case?
4. What would you actually do, and why?

Do not just analyze — take a position.

### Step 3: Compare to a Different Framework (150–250 words)

How would a different framework evaluate this same scenario? What would it say that your framework misses, and what does your framework see that the other one doesn't?

## Part 4 — Revision: Blind Spots and What Would Change Your Mind

**Goal:** Demonstrate intellectual honesty. Show that your framework is not a closed system but a position you hold under current evidence.

### Step 1: Identify Your Blind Spots (200–300 words)

What perspectives, communities, or harms might your framework systematically underweight?

Consider:
- Are there populations whose interests your framework tends to deprioritize?
- Are there kinds of harm (slow, diffuse, cultural, relational) that your framework handles less well?
- Are there cases where your framework might be used to justify something you would actually object to?

### Step 2: What Would Change Your Mind? (200–300 words)

Identify two or three things that could genuinely change your ethical conclusions:

- What evidence, if it emerged, would lead you to revise a core principle?
- What kind of case study or argument would most challenge your current framework?
- Have you encountered anything in this course that almost changed your mind — and what stopped it from doing so?

## Connection to Final Paper and Presentation

Your Personal Ethical Statement is not a separate document disconnected from your final work. It is the position from which your final paper argues.

Your final paper (Assignment 14) will ask you to analyze a system, make a claim, and defend it. The framework you develop here — your principles, your limits, your threshold for action — is the philosophical ground on which that argument stands.

Reviewers reading your final paper should be able to see your framework operating in your analysis, even if they have not read this assignment.
