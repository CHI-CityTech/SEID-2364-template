|  **Learning outcomes** | Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to: **Analyze** ethical issues arising in data science and machine intelligence, including AI, within complex, mediated environments that combine virtual, physical, societal, and institutional domains. **Apply** ethical approaches and analytical tools to evaluate decisions and actions within hybrid human–machine systems (including AI) operating in data-driven socio-technical contexts. **Evaluate** the ethical implications of data-driven and computational systems, as well as the policies that govern them, by assessing their impacts on stakeholders, power relations, trust, and institutional responsibility within society. **Develop and articulate** a personal ethical framework for responsible professional practice in environments shaped by data, algorithms, and human–machine interaction. **Communicate** ethical reasoning clearly and persuasively across diverse audiences and modalities, including written, oral, visual, and AI-mediated contexts. **Reflect** on how the design and modification of data-driven environments influence human behavior, social structures, and societal outcomes, revising ethical judgments in response to emerging complexity. |
|  **Detailed course content** | Data science and artificial intelligence increasingly shape how decisions are made in contemporary society, influencing domains such as employment, healthcare, finance, governance, and communication. Ethical responsibility emerges within data-driven systems through patterns of design, interaction, and decision-making that extend beyond purely technical concerns. Ethical challenges associated with data-intensive technologies include fairness and bias, privacy and surveillance, accountability, automation, and institutional governance. Ethics is examined not as a fixed set of rules, but as a contextual practice shaped by technological design, social interaction, and organizational structures. A central focus is placed on communication and mediation. Contemporary data science operates across multiple environments, including face-to-face collaboration, online interaction, automated decision systems, and AI-generated content. These environments are examined analytically to understand how ethical responsibilities shift depending on medium, distance, timing, and agency. Human and machine intelligences are examined as interacting components within complex socio-technical systems. Topics include the influence of algorithms on human behavior, the interpretation and trust of automated decisions, and the distribution of responsibility across individuals, institutions, and technological systems. Legal, cultural, and institutional perspectives are incorporated to illustrate how ethical expectations vary across contexts. Learning activities include case studies, structured discussions, collaborative projects, and reflective analysis. Work is conducted both individually and in teams, including structured interaction with AI tools, to examine ethically ambiguous situations rather than simplified or deterministic cases. These activities support the development of ethical reasoning skills and encourage critical engagement with differing perspectives. Upon completion, students are prepared to think responsibly about the systems they design, use, and influence, to have a framework with which to evaluate ethically complex or ambiguous environments, and recognize that ethical practice in data science is inseparable from how information moves through people, machines, and society. |

|  **Teaching approach** | The course employs an experiential and practice-based teaching approach that emphasizes active participation, reflection, and analysis within the socio-technical environments under study. Rather than relying primarily on lecture, instruction integrates discussion, case analysis, applied exercises, and reflective activities that situate ethical reasoning within lived and mediated contexts. Students engage directly in modes of interaction relevant to contemporary data-driven systems. These include participation in social media environments; collaborative teams composed of human and machine actors; interactions with agentic and automated systems; and both synchronous and asynchronous communication pathways. Through these engagements, students encounter ethical questions as they arise in practice, rather than solely through hypothetical or retrospective analysis. The course intentionally exposes students to multiple modes of communication, including text-based, mediated, and non-verbal interaction, as well as communication across different sensory domains. These experiences support analysis of how meaning, intent, responsibility, and trust are shaped by mediation, and how points of ethical risk or ambiguity can emerge from differences in representation, timing, or modality. Instruction is delivered through a combination of in-person, hybrid, and mediated activities. The intentional use of these modalities allows students to examine issues of access, inclusion, power, accountability, and participation as they arise within the learning environment itself. In this way, the modes of teaching and learning function as sites of ethical inquiry rather than as neutral delivery mechanisms. Collaborative learning is emphasized through group discussion and shared analysis, alongside individual reflection and ethical self-assessment. Students are encouraged to identify and evaluate ethical tensions within the environments they engage, articulate reasoned judgments, and refine their ethical reasoning through dialogue and feedback. Throughout the course, theoretical perspectives and analytical frameworks are introduced as tools to support ethical interpretation and decision-making. These frameworks are applied iteratively to experiential activities and case studies, enabling students to develop ethical reasoning skills that are adaptable to new, complex, and evolving socio-technical contexts. As this is the first offering of the course in this instructional format, aspects of activities, assignments, or projects may be adjusted during the semester in response to pedagogical considerations, emerging ethical questions, or the evolving learning context. All adjustments will be communicated clearly and in a timely manner. |

|  Assessment | Student performance in this course is assessed primarily through project-based and reflective work that evaluates ethical reasoning in complex societal and socio-technical contexts. Assessments emphasize analysis, articulation, and iterative refinement of ethical judgment, rather than memorization of predefined ethical rules. Readings and other learning materials are designed to support and inform project work. All assessments take place within the official semester dates and are graded in accordance with Modul University’s grading scale (minimum 60% required to pass). Assessment Components Applied Ethical Projects – 50% Students complete multiple applied projects throughout the semester focused on ethically analyzing data-driven or computational systems, policies, or socio-technical environments. Projects require students to identify ethical risks, map agency and responsibility, evaluate societal and institutional impacts, and articulate ethically informed positions. Projects may be individual or collaborative, as specified in assignment instructions. Personal Ethical Statement – 30% Each student develops an individual personal ethical statement that articulates their ethical assumptions, priorities, and decision-making approach in relation to data science, computation, and societal impact. This statement is developed iteratively over the semester and must demonstrate critical reflection, engagement with course concepts, and awareness of regulatory, professional, and societal considerations. Participation and Engagement – 20% Active participation in class discussions, collaborative activities, and experiential exercises. This component also includes short, low-stakes quizzes or check-ins used to support attendance tracking, engagement with course materials, and continuity of participation. Additional Notes Detailed descriptions, submission formats, and evaluation criteria for all assessments will be provided during the semester. Students may be asked to explain, discuss, or reflect on their submitted work as part of the assessment process. As this is the first offering of the course in this instructional format, specific project structures or sequencing may be refined in response to pedagogical needs or emerging ethical topics. Any such adjustments will be communicated clearly and in advance. |

|  **Use of artificial intelligence** | Artificial intelligence (AI) and related computational systems are a central subject of study in this course. In some course activities and assignments, students will be required to engage directly with AI systems as part of collaborative, analytical, or investigative work. In these contexts, AI systems may be examined not only as tools or objects of analysis, but also as collaborative agents whose roles, limitations, and ethical implications are critically assessed. Students may be required to work with AI systems when investigating topics such as bias, authorship, decision-making, accountability, or the ethical implications of automated and agentic systems. These activities support analysis of how AI systems participate in socio-technical processes, how they influence human judgment, and how ethical responsibility is distributed across hybrid human–machine arrangements. A central focus of this inquiry includes the ethics of attribution and the ethical division of labor in collaborative systems. Students examine how credit, responsibility, authorship, and accountability should be assigned when work is produced through combinations of human judgment and machine-generated contribution. This includes consideration of which tasks may be appropriately delegated to AI systems, which responsibilities must remain human, and how ethical transparency is maintained in collaborative workflows. The course recognizes that ethical frameworks must remain responsive to evolving technological capabilities. Students are encouraged to consider AI systems not only as they are currently deployed, but also in relation to future forms of machine intelligence whose capacities may challenge existing assumptions about agency, collaboration, labor, and responsibility. The concept of collaborative AI is therefore treated as a central and evolving area of ethical inquiry. The course also examines AI systems in relation to their situated context, including distinctions between personal or locally deployed models, institutionally managed systems, and large-scale corporate or platform-based AI services. Students analyze how factors such as control, scale, data access, governance, and opacity shape ethical outcomes, responsibility, and trust. When AI tools are used in coursework, their role must be transparent. Students may use AI systems to support exploratory thinking, comparative analysis, or reflection on alternative perspectives, provided that such use is explicitly acknowledged and does not replace the student’s own ethical reasoning, judgment, or authorship. Any use of AI tools must be disclosed, including a brief description of how the system was used and for what purpose. AI systems may not be used to generate substantive analytical content, complete assignments on behalf of the student, or produce work that is presented as the student’s own original reasoning without disclosure. Undisclosed or inappropriate use of AI constitutes academic misconduct and will be addressed in accordance with Modul University’s Examination Regulations and Student Code of Conduct. Because ethical reasoning is a core learning objective of the course, students may be asked to explain or defend their work, including their interactions with AI systems and the ethical decisions made regarding attribution, responsibility, and collaboration. The purpose of this policy is not to restrict engagement with AI, but to ensure that AI-supported work remains transparent, ethically grounded, and consistent with academic integrity and responsible professional practice. |
