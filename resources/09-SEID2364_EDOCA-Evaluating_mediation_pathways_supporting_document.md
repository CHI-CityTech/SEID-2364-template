# BBS Ethical Assessment Dimensions

## Effort, Distortion, Observability, Control, Authority (EDOCA)

### Framing Narrative

In the analysis of mediation pathways within the Balanced Blended Space (BBS) framework, it is not sufficient to identify where signals originate and terminate. To evaluate how systems behave—particularly under conditions of spatial distribution, degraded connectivity, variable access, and shifting authority—we need a set of ethical assessment dimensions.

This document introduces five such dimensions, organized as a causal mediation chain:

- **Effort / Energy** — The cost required to produce a change in system state (ΔD) at this point in the pathway.

- **Distortion** — The degree to which a signal is altered or preserved as it moves through the pathway, affecting its reliability and coherence.

- **Observability** — The degree to which an agent can perceive and interpret system state from available signals.

- **Control** — The ability of an agent to intervene in or modify system behavior at this point in the pathway.

- **Authority** — The legitimacy to assign, validate, or enforce decisions governing system behavior, including the ability to grant, restrict, or revoke system access.

These dimensions do not describe what a system is intended to do. Instead, they help assess what a system is capable of doing under actual conditions of mediation.

---

## 1. Effort / Energy

### Definition

Effort / Energy refers to the cost required to produce a change in system state (ΔD) at a given point in the mediation pathway.

This cost may be borne by different agents at different points in the mediation pathway, including shifts across space (local ↔ remote) and time (present ↔ future).

---

### Types of Cost

Effort / Energy can be analyzed through several distinct but related cost types. These are illustrative, not exhaustive. Additional forms of effort may emerge depending on the specific system and context.

#### 1. Economic / Monetary Cost (Primary Framing)

- Direct financial cost of system use (pricing, subscriptions, API usage)
- Cost of development, deployment, and maintenance
- Distribution of cost across stakeholders (who pays vs. who benefits)

Monetary cost is often the most visible and immediately understood form of cost, particularly within a capitalistic framework. It frequently serves as the primary lens through which systems are evaluated, even when it obscures deeper distributions of effort across agents, systems, environments, and future conditions.

#### 2. Time Cost

- Latency, delay, and waiting
- Retries and extended interaction cycles
- Time required to verify, correct, or recover from system behavior

Time cost reflects how mediation introduces delay and temporal fragmentation, especially across distributed systems.

#### 3. Cognitive Cost

- Mental effort required to interpret, decide, or compensate
- Increased uncertainty or ambiguity
- Attention required to monitor system behavior or detect failure

Cognitive cost increases as systems become less observable or more ambiguous, requiring interpretation rather than direct understanding.

#### 4. Computational / Infrastructure Cost

- Processing, bandwidth, storage, and routing
- Network maintenance, data transfer, and remote computation
- Often hidden from the user but critical to system function

Computational cost reflects the underlying work required to sustain mediation, often invisible to end users but essential to system operation.

#### 5. Physical / Operational Cost

- Material or embodied effort required to operate, maintain, or correct the system
- Physical infrastructure, hardware operation, device maintenance, or manual intervention
- Operational procedures required when automated or remote mediation is unavailable, degraded, or insufficient

Physical cost reflects how system behavior is ultimately grounded in material processes, whether automated or human-driven.

#### 6. Environmental Cost

- Energy consumption of computational systems (e.g., data centers, GPUs)
- Heat generation and cooling requirements
- Resource extraction and material impact across the hardware lifecycle
- Contribution to global energy demand and carbon emissions
- Long-term impacts on the Earth’s climate system, including global warming and environmental degradation

This cost is often invisible at the user level but has significant long-term and large-scale implications, particularly at the level of planetary systems.

#### 7. Societal / Institutional Cost

- Human labor embedded in system creation and maintenance (e.g., data labeling, moderation, compliance)
- Policy, governance, legal, and regulatory overhead
- Cultural and organizational infrastructure required to sustain the system
- Unequal distribution of burden across communities or institutions

Societal cost reflects how systems rely on and redistribute effort across human, organizational, and institutional structures.

#### 8. Opportunity Cost

- Loss of alternative actions
- Reduced capability due to system limitations
- Resources directed toward one pathway instead of another

Opportunity cost reflects what is no longer possible as a result of committing to a particular mediation pathway.

These categories are intended as guides for analysis, not a fixed taxonomy.

---

### Key Questions

- What types of cost are present at this point in the pathway?
- Who bears these costs?
- How are these costs redistributed as spatial distance changes?
- Are any costs hidden, delayed, or shifted to other agents, systems, institutions, or future conditions?

---

### Observations

- Remote mediation often redistributes cost rather than eliminating it.
- Systems may reduce local human effort while increasing hidden computational, institutional, economic, or environmental cost.
- Under degraded or unavailable conditions, cost often shifts back toward local agents.

---

### Example (Flight Scenario)

- Full connectivity: low cognitive and physical cost for the traveler, high infrastructure support.
- Degraded connectivity: increased cognitive cost, time cost, and uncertainty.
- No connectivity: the remote system becomes unreachable, and decision-making shifts toward local agents and available local resources.

---

## 2. Distortion

### Definition

Distortion refers to the degree to which a signal is altered as it moves through a mediation pathway. Fidelity refers to the degree to which the signal is preserved. Together, these describe the signal’s integrity.

BBS does not model meaning itself. However, it does model signal degradation across physical, virtual, and cognitive spaces, because degradation affects the likelihood that meaning remains coherent between source and destination agents.

---

### Key Questions

- Where does the signal change form?
- How is the signal transformed (e.g., preserved, compressed, abstracted, delayed)?
- To what degree does the signal remain usable for the next agent or system in the pathway (e.g., unusable → partially usable → fully usable)?
- To what extent does the system continue to act as signal quality degrades (e.g., halts → proceeds with safeguards → proceeds without safeguards)?

---

### Common Sources of Distortion

- Noise or interference
- Ambiguity in natural language
- Translation between domains (e.g., human language to computational representation)
- Compression or abstraction
- Representation mismatch
- Incomplete or delayed feedback
- State divergence between an internal model and external system conditions

- Bias in data, models, or processes, including:
  - Sampling bias (who/what is included or excluded)
  - Historical bias (patterns reflecting past inequities)
  - Measurement bias (how variables are defined or captured)
  - Labeling/annotation bias (subjective or inconsistent ground truth)
  - Aggregation bias (using one model across heterogeneous groups)

---

### Observations

- Distortion is not always harmful; transformation is often necessary for mediation.
- Distortion becomes ethically significant when degraded signals continue to guide consequential action.
- Systems do not fail only when they stop working; they may also fail when they continue acting on unreliable signals.

---

### Example (Flight Scenario)

- Full connectivity: signal may remain relatively coherent.
- Degraded connectivity: signal may become partial, delayed, or incomplete.
- No connectivity: the remote signal cannot participate in the active mediation pathway.

---

## 3. Observability

### Definition

Observability is the degree to which an agent can perceive, interpret, and understand the state of a system through available signals. This is often discussed in related terms such as visibility or transparency, though observability is broader and includes both what can be seen and how it can be interpreted.

---

### Key Distinction

- **Access**: Can a connection or mediation pathway be established?
- **Observability**: Can the system state be meaningfully understood through the available signal?

Access is a precondition for observability, but does not guarantee it. In the limiting case:

> **No access ⇒ Observability = 0**

Within the BBS spatial framework, loss of access also implies a shift in spatial classification:

> **No access ⇒ Remote space becomes Unreachable space**

However, the inverse is not true: systems may be accessible but still poorly observable due to degradation, abstraction, opacity, or misleading feedback.

---

### Key Questions

- What information is available at this point in the pathway?
- Is the information complete, partial, delayed, or misleading?
- How does spatial distance affect visibility into the system?
- What is visible to one agent but hidden from another?

---

### Observations

- Observability is graded, not binary.
- Increased distance often introduces latency, abstraction, and black-box behavior.
- Systems can remain accessible while becoming effectively opaque.
- Poor observability increases the chance that agents act on incomplete or degraded signals.

---

### Example (Flight Scenario)

- WiFi available: access exists.
- Low bandwidth: observability is partial or degraded.
- System outputs may be delayed, incomplete, or unreliable.

---

## 4. Control

### Definition

Control refers to the ability of an agent to intervene in, modify, or redirect the behavior of a system at a given point in the mediation pathway.

---

### Key Questions

- Who can change the system’s behavior?
- Where does control shift as spatial distance changes?
- Is control centralized, distributed, fragmented, or local?
- What actions are possible under full, degraded, or unavailable access?

---

### Observations

- Control often shifts away from local agents as systems become more remote.
- Under degraded or unavailable conditions, control may revert toward local agents.
- Control can be fragmented, with different agents controlling different parts of the pathway.
- Control may remain active even when observability is poor, which can increase ethical risk.

---

### Example (Flight Scenario)

- Normal operation: control may be distributed among pilot, ATC, airline systems, navigation systems, and automated systems.
- Degraded connectivity: some control remains remote, but local agents must compensate.
- Loss of connectivity: control shifts toward local agents and local systems.

---

## 5. Authority

### Definition

Authority is the capacity of a system, agent, or institution to assign, validate, or enforce decisions within a mediation pathway. Authority includes the ability to grant, restrict, or revoke access, and to determine which agents are permitted to exercise control.

---

### Key Clarification

Authority is not the same as control.

- **Control**: the ability to act or produce a change in system state.
- **Authority**: the legitimacy to determine who should act, how action should occur, and whether an action is valid.

These may diverge.

---

### Key Questions

- Who is recognized as the decision-maker?
- Who grants or restricts access?
- Where is decision legitimacy located?
- Does authority remain aligned with control?
- What happens when authority persists but access, observability, or control degrade?

---

### Observations

- Authority often remains remote even when control shifts locally.
- Systems may continue to assert authority even when they lack capability.
- Misalignment between authority and control is a key source of ethical risk.
- Authority without sufficient observability may validate decisions based on degraded signals.

---

### Example (Flight Scenario)

- Remote systems and institutions (ATC, airline systems, regulatory structures) may retain authority.
- In degraded conditions, local agents may need to act even when formal authority remains elsewhere.
- This creates potential tension between actual control and recognized authority.

---

## Cross-Dimensional Interaction (EDOCA Chain)

These dimensions form a causal chain, not independent categories:

- Effort / Energy produces or enables transformation.
- Transformation may introduce Distortion.
- Distortion affects Observability.
- Observability conditions Control.
- Control operates within, against, or under Authority.

This chain is not rigid or one-directional in all cases, but it provides a useful starting point for ethical analysis.

---

### Alignment vs. Divergence

A system is relatively stable when:

- Effort, distortion, observability, control, and authority remain aligned.

A system becomes unstable when:

- One or more dimensions diverge.

---

### Example Patterns

#### Case 1: No Access

- Effort: shifts local
- Distortion: remote signal unavailable
- Observability: 0 for the remote system
- Control: local
- Authority: may remain remote or become ambiguous

→ The remote space becomes Unreachable space in the active pathway.

#### Case 2: Access + High Distortion

- Effort: moderate or increased
- Distortion: high
- Observability: degraded
- Control: partial or misdirected
- Authority: may remain remote

→ High risk due to misinterpretation or unreliable action.

#### Case 3: Authority without Capability

- Effort: low for local agents, high elsewhere or hidden
- Distortion: unclear or unrecognized
- Observability: limited
- Control: constrained or misaligned
- Authority: remote or institutionally retained

→ Ethical instability emerges when authority exceeds actual capability.

---

## Core Principle

A system should be evaluated not by its intended function, but by what it is capable of doing under real conditions of mediation.

---

## Summary

The EDOCA framework provides an ethical assessment lens for analyzing mediation pathways in BBS:

- Effort reveals where cost is produced, borne, or redistributed.
- Distortion reveals how signal integrity changes.
- Observability reveals what can be known from available signals.
- Control reveals who can act.
- Authority reveals who is recognized as able to assign, validate, or govern action.

Together, these dimensions allow for a structured analysis of how spatial distance, system access, signal degradation, and authority shape ethical risk.
