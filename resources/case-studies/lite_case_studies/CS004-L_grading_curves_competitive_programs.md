# Case Study (Lite): Grading Curves in Competitive Academic Programs
## Performance Signaling, Fairness, and Structural Incentives

This lite case provides shared factual grounding while leaving core ethical and governance analysis to you. For universal expectations on final deliverable depth, see `../SEID2364_General_Case_Study_Instructions_for_Students.md`.

## Scenario
In a competitive program, grades are curved so that only a fixed proportion of students can receive top marks regardless of absolute performance. Institutions use curves to preserve ranking differentiation for scholarships and selection decisions. Students report high stress, reduced collaboration, and concerns about fairness under relative scoring systems.

The central challenge is whether curve-based ranking advances legitimate institutional goals without generating avoidable inequity and harm.

## Core Questions for Analysis
1. Which stakeholders benefit from relative ranking, and which are harmed by forced distribution constraints?
2. How do grading rules mediate long-term opportunity pathways beyond the classroom?
3. What harms arise under strict curves, and what harms arise when ranking differentiation is removed?
4. How should institutions evaluate fairness when calibration, transparency, and external signaling pressures conflict?
5. What governance mechanisms can reduce harm while preserving credible performance standards?

## Required Student Analysis Outputs
1. Pathway map from grading design to outcomes in scholarships, hiring, and student behavior.
2. Stakeholder and harms matrix with bidirectional and tradeoff risk analysis.
3. Ethics comparison memo using at least two frameworks and one governance lens.
4. Policy recommendation covering transparency, review process, and equity safeguards.
