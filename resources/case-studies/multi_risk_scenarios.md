# Multi-Risk Scenarios: Cases Combining Different Types of Harm

The following scenarios each involve multiple, overlapping types of harm. They are intended to support analysis of how physical, psychological, social, economic, and systemic harms interact and are distributed across different stakeholders.

---

## 1. Emergency Room Triage During Resource Scarcity

A hospital emergency department has more critically ill patients than available ICU beds. Medical staff must decide which patients receive intensive care and which receive less aggressive treatment or are redirected elsewhere. Patients differ in age, overall health, likelihood of recovery, and immediacy of need. Families, clinicians, and administrators are all affected by these decisions.

This situation involves multiple types of harm, including physical harm to patients who do not receive care, emotional harm to families, and moral or psychological harm to healthcare workers required to make life-and-death decisions. Harms may affect individuals directly while also shaping broader trust in the healthcare system.

**Types of harm present:**
- Physical harm (patients who do not receive intensive care)
- Emotional/psychological harm (families, healthcare workers)
- Moral injury (clinicians facing triage decisions)
- Systemic harm (erosion of public trust in healthcare)

---

## 2. Mandatory Vaccination Policy for Public School Attendance

A city requires all students attending public schools to receive certain vaccinations unless they qualify for limited medical exemptions. The policy is intended to reduce the spread of contagious disease and protect public health. Some families object to the mandate based on personal beliefs or concerns about medical risk.

Potential harms include health risks or anxiety experienced by individual children and families, as well as broader societal harms if vaccination rates fall and disease outbreaks occur. The situation highlights tension between individual autonomy and collective safety, as well as between perceived and statistical forms of harm.

**Types of harm present:**
- Physical harm (risk of adverse reactions; risk of disease if rates fall)
- Psychological harm (anxiety among objecting families)
- Autonomy harm (restriction of individual and parental choice)
- Societal harm (potential disease outbreak if compliance is low)

---

## 3. Eminent Domain for Infrastructure Development

A government plans to build a new highway or transit line intended to improve transportation efficiency and economic growth. To do so, it exercises eminent domain to acquire private homes and businesses located along the proposed route. Property owners receive financial compensation but must relocate.

This situation involves economic harm to displaced individuals, social and community harm from neighborhood disruption, and potential long-term societal benefits such as reduced congestion or increased access. Harms are unevenly distributed, affecting specific individuals immediately while benefits are dispersed across a larger population.

**Types of harm present:**
- Economic harm (displacement costs, undervalued compensation)
- Social/community harm (disruption of neighborhoods, loss of place)
- Psychological harm (stress of forced relocation)
- Distributional harm (concentrated costs, diffuse benefits)

---

## 4. Academic Grading Curves in Competitive Programs

In a highly competitive academic program, grades are assigned using a strict curve. Only a fixed percentage of students can receive top grades regardless of overall performance. This system is used to maintain standards and distinguish candidates for scholarships or advanced opportunities.

Possible harms include psychological stress, damaged collaboration among students, and long-term consequences for individuals whose grades affect career prospects. At the same time, the system may be seen as serving institutional or societal goals related to credentialing, selection, and perceived fairness.

**Types of harm present:**
- Psychological harm (stress, anxiety, damaged peer relationships)
- Economic/opportunity harm (career prospects tied to relative rank)
- Social harm (erosion of collaborative learning culture)
- Systemic harm (structural incentives that may disadvantage certain groups)

---

## Discussion Prompts

1. In each scenario, who bears the most immediate harm and who bears the most diffuse or delayed harm?
2. How does the distinction between *intended* and *unintended* harm apply across these cases?
3. Which stakeholders have the most power to shape outcomes, and how does that affect moral responsibility?
4. How would different ethical frameworks (utilitarian, deontological, virtue ethics) evaluate the tradeoffs in each case?
5. Are there harms in these scenarios that are invisible or underweighted? How might they be made more visible?
