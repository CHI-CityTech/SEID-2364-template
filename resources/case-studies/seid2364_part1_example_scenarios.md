
# SEID 2364 – Part 1 Example Scenarios

The following are **example situations** intended to illustrate the *scope and level of description* expected in **Part 1** of Assignment 4.

Each example:
- Describes a plausible real‑world decision context
- Indicates that **different types of harm may arise or conflict**
- Does **not** evaluate, resolve, or judge the situation

Students may **select one of these situations** or **define their own**, provided it is similar in scope.

---

## Example 1: Emergency Room Triage During Resource Scarcity

A hospital emergency department has more critically ill patients than available ICU beds. Medical staff must decide which patients receive intensive care and which receive less aggressive treatment or are redirected elsewhere. Patients differ in age, overall health, likelihood of recovery, and immediacy of need. Families, clinicians, and administrators are all affected by these decisions.

This situation involves multiple types of harm, including physical harm to patients who do not receive care, emotional harm to families, and moral or psychological harm to healthcare workers required to make life‑and‑death decisions. Harms may affect individuals directly while also shaping broader trust in the healthcare system.

---

## Example 2: Mandatory Vaccination for Public School Attendance

A city requires all students attending public schools to receive certain vaccinations unless they qualify for limited medical exemptions. The policy is intended to reduce the spread of contagious disease and protect public health. Some families object to the mandate based on personal beliefs or concerns about medical risk.

Potential harms include health risks or anxiety experienced by individual children and families, as well as broader societal harms if vaccination rates fall and disease outbreaks occur. The situation highlights tension between individual autonomy and collective safety, as well as between perceived and statistical forms of harm.

---

## Example 3: Eminent Domain for Infrastructure Development

A government plans to build a new highway or transit line intended to improve transportation efficiency and economic growth. To do so, it exercises eminent domain to acquire private homes and businesses located along the proposed route. Property owners receive financial compensation but must relocate.

This situation involves economic harm to displaced individuals, social and community harm from neighborhood disruption, and potential long‑term societal benefits such as improved mobility or access. Harms are unevenly distributed, affecting specific individuals immediately while benefits are dispersed across a larger population.

---

## Example 4: Grading Curves in Competitive Academic Programs

In a highly competitive academic program, grades are assigned using a strict curve. Only a fixed percentage of students can receive top grades regardless of overall performance. This system is used to maintain standards and to distinguish candidates for scholarships, internships, or advanced opportunities.

Possible harms include psychological stress, reduced collaboration among students, and long‑term consequences for individuals whose grades affect career prospects. At the same time, the system may be viewed as serving institutional or societal goals related to selection, signaling, and credentialing.

---

### Note to Students

These examples are provided to clarify **scope**, not to prescribe topics. You may select one of these situations or propose your own, as long as it involves a real or plausible decision context where different kinds of harm may arise or conflict.
