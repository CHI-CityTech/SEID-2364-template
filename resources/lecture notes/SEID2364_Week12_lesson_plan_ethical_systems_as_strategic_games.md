# V3 – SEID 2364 Week 12 Lesson Plan
## Ethical Systems as Strategic Games

## Topic
Game Theory, Ethical Systems, and Final Case Study Preparation

## Framing Narrative

Week 12 functions as a synthesis and launch session. Students have already encountered ethical frameworks, BBS mediation pathways, bias, risk, trust, space, time, EDOCA, professional codes, institutional governance, and the distinction between legal compliance and ethical responsibility. Week 11 introduced ACM and IEEE professional codes through the COVID-19 misinformation moderation case. Week 12 now introduces game theory as a formal vocabulary for describing strategic ethical systems.

The goal is not to teach game theory as a mathematical field in depth or to solve games through equilibrium analysis. The goal is to help students recognize that ethical problems in data science often emerge from strategic interaction among agents operating under rules, constraints, incentives, partial information, unequal authority, and uneven control over signals. Game theory provides the strategic vocabulary. BBS and EDOCA provide the ethical and mediation vocabulary.

This session also reconnects to an earlier class discussion in which Chess, Stratego, Poker, dice, and Austerlitz were used to distinguish kinds of information visibility. That prior discussion becomes the bridge into game theory. Students have already seen that systems differ by what is visible, what is hidden, what is revealed only through interaction, what is random, and what may not yet exist. Week 12 extends that insight into final case-study analysis.

The session should remain concrete. Students do not need to complete or play a fully designed game. They need to learn a reusable structure for specifying an ethical system: who the agents are, what each agent wants, what signals move through the system, what information is visible or hidden, how rules and meta-rules shape behavior, and where local success produces broader harm.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the session, students should be able to:

- Explain what game theory means by a “game” as a formal model of strategic interaction.
- Identify strategic interdependence among agents in an ethical case.
- Distinguish system goals, player goals, victory conditions, and ethical conditions.
- Describe how signals can be generated, amplified, verified, distorted, suppressed, or authorized.
- Recognize how misinformation, disinformation, malinformation, and false information can function as signals, strategies, distortions, cheating, or rule exploitation.
- Distinguish the internal game from the larger meta-game in which enforcement, cheating, incentives, and governance operate.
- Begin a game-theoretic specification of a case study using a short reusable structure.
- Select or tentatively identify a Lite Case Study for the final paper.
- Assess whether their current Zotero sources provide adequate ethical evidence infrastructure.
- Reflect on how case-study analysis will support the development of a personal and professional ethical framework.

## Session Structure

## Phase 1 – Mini-Lecture: Game Theory as Strategic Interdependence
**Approximate time: 20–25 minutes**

Keep this concise, but treat it as the conceptual center of the class. The goal is vocabulary and historical orientation, not mathematical depth.

Begin with origins. Game theory emerged as a formal way to analyze strategic decision-making, especially in situations where the outcome of one actor’s choice depends on the choices of others. Its modern development is often associated with mathematics, economics, military strategy, bargaining, and decision theory. In this class, students do not need to calculate equilibria or solve payoff matrices. They need to understand why the word “game” refers to a formalized strategic environment rather than entertainment.

Key explanation:

Game theory studies strategic interaction. In this context, a “game” means a formal model of a situation in which multiple agents make choices under rules, constraints, incentives, uncertainty, and partial information. A game becomes ethically significant when one agent’s action changes the risks, opportunities, harms, or outcomes available to others.

Key terms:

- Player / agent: who can act.
- Strategy / action: what the agent can do.
- Goal: what the agent wants.
- Victory condition: what counts as success for that agent.
- Information state: what the agent can know, observe, infer, or misunderstand.
- Rules: what actions are allowed, blocked, costly, rewarded, delayed, or punished.
- Payoff / incentive: what the agent gains, avoids, protects, or risks.
- Turn structure: when and how often agents can act.
- Outcome: what changes in the system.
- Strategic interdependence: the condition in which each agent’s actions affect the options or outcomes of other agents.

Course connection:

- Game theory provides strategic vocabulary: agents, goals, strategies, incentives, information states, and outcomes.
- BBS describes agents, signals, spaces, pathways, and transformations.
- EDOCA diagnoses effort, distortion, observability, control, and authority.
- Professional codes help evaluate what responsible actors should do.

Explicit bridge to prior class:

Students have already used games to discuss observability. Chess showed open board state but hidden strategy. Stratego showed visible position but hidden capability. Poker showed hidden state and permitted deception. Dice showed a future state that does not yet exist when the player must decide. Austerlitz showed that opacity can be manufactured in a real strategic system. Week 12 uses these earlier examples to move from informal comparison to formal case-study specification.

Core statement for the board:

> Game theory helps us describe the strategic structure of an ethical system. BBS and EDOCA help us analyze how that structure distributes harm, trust, responsibility, and authority.

## Phase 2 – Warm-Up: Games as Information Structures
**Approximate time: 25–30 minutes**

The instructor briefly revisits the earlier game examples and explicitly connects them to observability, partial information, deception, and ethical risk. The point is not to classify games for their own sake. The point is to show that different information structures create different ethical and strategic conditions.

Use the examples as a progression from open state to hidden state to manufactured opacity.

### Example 1 – Chess: Open State, Hidden Strategy

Prompts:

- What is visible to both players?
- What remains hidden even though the board is visible?
- Is deception possible if all pieces are visible?
- Where would cheating occur: inside the board rules or outside them?

Teaching point:

Chess has open board state but hidden strategy. Players can see all pieces and legal moves, but not the opponent’s plan, risk tolerance, tactical intention, or long-term strategy. This distinguishes visible state from hidden intent.

### Example 2 – Stratego: Visible Position, Hidden Capability

Prompts:

- What can you see?
- What can you not see?
- How is hidden capability revealed?
- Why does interaction increase knowledge but also increase risk?

Teaching point:

Stratego has visible positions but hidden identities and capabilities. You know where the opponent’s pieces are, but not what they are. Rank and power are revealed through interaction. This models partial observability and capability uncertainty.

### Example 3 – Poker: Hidden State and Permitted Deception

Prompts:

- What information is hidden?
- Is bluffing cheating?
- What makes deception legal inside one game but unethical in another system?

Teaching point:

Poker permits concealment and bluffing within the rules. This allows students to distinguish bad information as a permitted strategy from bad information as cheating, fraud, or unethical distortion.

### Example 4 – Dice / Craps: Future State Not Yet Determined

Prompts:

- What information is unknown because it is hidden?
- What information is unknown because it does not yet exist?
- How does this differ from drawing cards from a deck?

Teaching point:

In dice games, the outcome does not exist until the roll occurs. This helps students distinguish hidden fixed information from genuinely indeterminate future information.

### Example 5 – Austerlitz: Manufactured Opacity in a Real Strategic System

Prompts:

- What did Napoleon make visible?
- What did he hide?
- How did the opposing side misread the game they were playing?
- How does manufactured opacity change risk, effort, control, and authority?

Teaching point:

Austerlitz moves the discussion from bounded games to real strategic systems. The opposing side believed it had enough information to act, but Napoleon manufactured opacity by concealing capability and manipulating apparent weakness. In course terms: the Allies thought they were playing Chess, but Napoleon made it Stratego.

Instructor synthesis:

> Games help us see information structure. Ethical systems depend on what is visible, what is hidden, what is revealed through interaction, what is random, what is delayed, and what can be strategically concealed.

Transition:

> Bounded games are useful because their rules, pieces, and spaces are constrained. Real data systems are harder because the game may not be bounded, the rules may change, and actors may enter from the meta-game.

## Phase 3 – Bad Information, Internal Game, and Meta-Game
**Approximate time: 10–12 minutes**

The instructor connects simple games to data science and misinformation.

Key distinctions:

- Misinformation: false or inaccurate information that may spread without intent to deceive.
- Disinformation: intentionally deceptive or strategically manipulative information.
- Malinformation: real information deployed in a harmful, misleading, or context-collapsing way.
- False information: incorrect information whose ethical significance depends on how it moves, who relies on it, and what harm it produces.

Explain that bad information may function as:

- a signal type;
- a player strategy;
- a distortion introduced during mediation;
- a weakness in observability;
- a form of cheating;
- a rule exploit;
- an external meta-game intervention.

Internal game / meta-game distinction:

The internal game consists of official rules, permitted moves, visible state, and stated victory conditions. The meta-game is the larger system in which the game is played, monitored, enforced, exploited, or corrupted.

Example:

Tournament chess includes not only the board game, but clocks, ratings, referees, anti-cheating procedures, spectators, prize money, institutional rules, and reputational consequences. Cheating often occurs outside the legal moves of the board game but inside the larger tournament system.

Data science connection:

A platform may prohibit bot manipulation internally, but bad actors may operate through fake accounts, cross-platform coordination, timing attacks, identity laundering, or external funding. Ethical analysis must therefore ask how the larger system enables, detects, discourages, punishes, or rewards rule evasion.

## Phase 4 – Shared Construction: Game-Theoretic Specification of COVID Misinformation Moderation
**Approximate time: 35–45 minutes**

Use one shared case. Do not split the class into groups. Treat the five students as one design team.

Recommended shared case:

COVID misinformation moderation, continuing from Week 11.

Simplified scenario:

A platform is receiving rapidly spreading posts about a public health crisis. Some posts are accurate, some are uncertain, some are misleading, and some are intentionally manipulative. Moderation is possible, but every intervention carries costs. Over-removal may suppress legitimate speech. Under-removal may amplify harm. Transparency may help users but may also reveal moderation rules to bad actors. Delay may increase damage.

### Short Reusable Specification Structure

Use this structure on the board. This is the version students should be able to reuse in the final assignment.

1. System Goal  
What does the system claim to accomplish?

2. Agents / Players  
Who can act within or upon the system?

3. Player Goals and Victory Conditions  
What does each agent want? What counts as success for that agent?

4. Signals and Signal Powers  
What moves through the system? Who can generate, amplify, verify, distort, suppress, authorize, or reinterpret signals?

5. Rules, Constraints, and Meta-Game  
What actions are allowed, blocked, costly, delayed, rewarded, or punished? What larger system surrounds the internal game?

6. Information and Observability  
What is visible, hidden, partially known, delayed, or distorted? Who can see what?

7. Action Frequency, Control, and Authority  
Who acts quickly? Who can act multiple times? Who acts slowly? Who can change the rules rather than merely act within them?

8. Ethical Instability and Failure Mode  
Where can a player satisfy a local victory condition while producing system-level harm?

9. Possible Redesign or Intervention  
What rule, incentive, signal pathway, authority structure, or observability condition could be changed?

10. Unresolved Questions  
What can the class not yet define? Which assumptions remain contested?

### Suggested Agents for the Shared Case

- Platform governance team
- Public health institution
- Ordinary user / community member
- Bad actor or strategic misinformation producer
- Regulator or professional oversight body
- Optional non-player system component: recommender algorithm

Instructor note:

The recommender may be treated either as an agent or as a mediation structure. If students disagree, preserve the disagreement. The question itself is analytically useful.

### Board Output

The class should produce a partial specification, not a finished game. The board should contain at minimum:

- system goal;
- agents;
- at least three agent goals;
- at least three victory conditions;
- at least three signal types;
- at least one fast actor and one slow authority actor;
- at least one meta-game vulnerability;
- at least one ethical instability;
- at least three unresolved questions.

Instructor emphasis:

> The incompleteness is not failure. The unresolved questions show where ethical analysis must continue.

## Phase 5 – Reset and Final Paper Orientation
**Approximate time: 15–20 minutes**

After the shared case specification, the instructor reviews where the class is in the semester and how the method just practiced leads into the final paper. This reset should now feel grounded: students have seen the structure before being asked to locate their own progress.

Students identify:

- one assignment or concept they feel confident about;
- one assignment or concept that remains incomplete or unclear;
- one Lite Case Study they may want to use for the final paper;
- one source gap they already know they need to address.

- weak or missing sources;
- confusion between legal compliance and ethical responsibility;
- unclear use of BBS or EDOCA;
- shallow stakeholder analysis;
- uncertainty about professional codes;
- final case selections that may be too broad or too vague.

Transition statement:

> The final paper will not simply ask whether a system is good or bad. It will ask how ethical conditions are produced by the structure of the system.

## Phase 7 – Zotero as Ethical Evidence Infrastructure
**Approximate time: 20–25 minutes**

Frame Zotero not only as a citation tool, but as ethical evidence infrastructure. Poor sources produce weak ethical claims. Missing sources create distortion. Overreliance on platform rhetoric or journalism without verification can undermine trust. A final ethical analysis must be supported by a source ecology that includes technical, policy, stakeholder, and scholarly perspectives where possible.

Students review their Zotero entries and identify whether they currently have:

- one source establishing the factual system context;
- one source addressing ethical or social consequences;
- one source addressing governance, regulation, or professional standards;
- one source that complicates or challenges their initial position;
- sufficient bibliographic information and notes to use the source later.

In-class action:

Each student improves at least one Zotero entry by adding:

- a short summary;
- why the source is relevant;
- what claim it might support;
- what limitation or bias the source may have.

Instructor may review one entry with the class to show the difference between a weak citation and a useful evidence note.

## Phase 8 – Assignment Introduction: Final Case Study Specification
**Approximate time: 10–15 minutes**

Introduce the next assignment in short form. This assignment begins the final paper process. It does not require the full paper yet.

### Assignment 12 – Final Case Study Specification Draft

Students will select one Lite Case Study from the provided packet and produce a short specification draft that prepares them for the final paper.

Working file name:

`assignment12-case-specification.md`

Suggested location:

`/students/lastname-firstname/assignments/assignment12-case-specification.md`

### Short Assignment Description

Using one Lite Case Study from the provided packet, construct a preliminary game-theoretic and ethical specification of the case. The purpose is to identify the structure of the ethical problem before writing the final paper.

Students should address:

1. Case selected  
Identify the Lite Case Study and briefly describe the system.

2. System goal  
What does the system claim to accomplish?

3. Agents / players  
Who can act within or upon the system?

4. Player goals and victory conditions  
What does at least three agents want? What would count as success for each?

5. Signals and signal powers  
What information, data, claims, scores, warnings, recommendations, or outputs move through the system? Who can generate, amplify, verify, distort, suppress, or authorize them?

6. Rules, constraints, and meta-game  
What actions are allowed, constrained, hidden, delayed, rewarded, or punished? What larger system surrounds the internal game?

7. Information and observability  
What is visible, hidden, partially known, delayed, or distorted?

8. EDOCA diagnosis  
Identify at least one issue involving effort, distortion, observability, control, or authority.

9. Ethical instability  
Where might one agent achieve a local victory condition while producing broader harm?

10. Evidence plan  
List at least three sources needed for the final paper and identify what kind of evidence each source provides.

11. Unresolved question  
Identify one question that remains genuinely unresolved and will require further research or analysis.

### Minimum Requirements

- 800–1200 words or equivalent structured Markdown
- At least three Zotero sources identified
- At least one source must be scholarly, policy-based, technical, or institutional rather than only journalistic
- Must include the short specification headings above
- Must end with one unresolved ethical question

### Purpose of the Assignment

This is not the final argument. It is the structural preparation for the final argument. Students are expected to discover ambiguity, missing information, and unresolved tensions.

## Phase 9 – Closing Reflection: Toward Personal and Professional Ethics
**Approximate time: 10–12 minutes**

The session ends with reflection rather than opening provocation. This reflection should look forward to one of the final course tasks: developing a personal and professional ethical framework.

Students respond privately:

1. What ethical instability in your possible final case seems most difficult to resolve, even after formal analysis?
2. What does that instability reveal about your own personal ethical commitments?
3. What would a responsible professional be obligated to do in this situation, even if the system rewards a different behavior?

Optional fourth prompt:

> What information would you need in order to make your analysis more trustworthy?

Purpose:

This reflection connects the case-study assignment to the larger end-of-semester task of articulating a personal and professional ethical theory. Students should begin recognizing that their final paper is not only about an external case. It is also evidence of how they reason ethically as emerging data-science professionals.

Collect or ask students to retain this reflection for their assignment draft.

## Assignment Bridge

Assignment 12 prepares the final paper by forcing students to define the structure of the ethical system before making an ethical claim. The final paper will develop this specification into a full analysis using course frameworks, evidence, and a reasoned conclusion.

The closing reflection also prepares a later assignment in which students articulate a personal and professional ethical framework. The case study provides the external system; the personal/professional framework asks how the student would reason and act within such a system.

## Instructor Notes

- Keep the game theory mini-lecture short. The activity matters more than the terminology.
- Do not let the class try to build a complete simulation.
- Preserve disagreement about agents, goals, and signals when it reveals real ambiguity.
- Push students toward specificity: which agent, which signal, which rule, which authority?
- Use COVID misinformation as the shared model only long enough to teach the method.
- Use Chess, Stratego, Poker, dice, and Austerlitz as continuity examples from the earlier observability lecture.
- Emphasize that bounded games clarify structure, but real data systems exceed games because rules may change, actors may enter from outside, and enforcement belongs to a larger meta-game.
- Do not allow Lite Case selection to become open-topic brainstorming.
- Treat Zotero as part of ethical reasoning, not as clerical cleanup.

## Potential Failure Modes

- Students treat the activity as role-playing rather than formal specification.
- The class spends too much time debating COVID policy rather than modeling the system.
- Students get absorbed in game examples and do not transition to data-science systems.
- Students define goals too generally, such as “do good” or “avoid harm.”
- Victory conditions are confused with ethical conditions.
- Signals are treated as content only, rather than as objects moving through pathways.
- Cheating is treated only as individual bad behavior rather than as a meta-game design problem.
- Students choose final cases that are too broad or lack researchable evidence.
- Zotero work becomes mechanical rather than connected to evidentiary trust.

## Optional Extension

If time remains, ask each student to name one rule change that would make ethical behavior easier in their selected Lite Case Study. Then ask whether that rule change creates a new problem for another agent.

## Student-Facing Session Title

Ethical Systems as Strategic Games: Agents, Incentives, Signals, and Responsibility

## Student-Facing Short Description

This week we will use the idea of a “game” in its formal sense: a structured environment in which actors make decisions under rules, constraints, incentives, goals, and incomplete information. We will reconnect to earlier examples such as Chess, Stratego, Poker, dice, and Austerlitz to examine different kinds of visibility, hidden information, deception, and strategic uncertainty. Then we will begin constructing a simplified game-theoretic specification from a real case study, using it to analyze how ethical problems emerge in data-driven systems. We may not finish the game; instead, we will identify the agents, signals, goals, victory conditions, asymmetries, and unresolved questions that the model reveals. Finally, we will apply the same logic to selecting and planning the final case-study assignment and begin reflecting on personal and professional ethical responsibility.
