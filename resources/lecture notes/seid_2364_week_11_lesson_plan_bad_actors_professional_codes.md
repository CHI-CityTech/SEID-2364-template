# SEID 2364 – Week 11 Lesson Plan
## Applied Ethical Analysis: Bad Actors, Professional Codes, and Framework Disagreement

### Course Context
This lesson continues the course transition from foundational ethical theory toward applied ethical analysis within complex socio-technical systems. Earlier weeks introduced students to deontology, consequentialism, virtue ethics, stakeholder analysis, the EU AI Act, BBS mediation pathways, trust, temporal and spatial distance, and EDOCA.

Week 11 assumes that students are already familiar with major ethical traditions. The purpose is not to reteach ethical theory. Instead, the lesson asks students to examine how ethical priorities become operationalized within professional organizations, governance systems, and socio-technical infrastructures.

The lesson is anchored in a shared case: **COVID-19 misinformation moderation**. The case is introduced only through a short case card at the beginning of class. Students first respond using their existing ethical vocabulary. Only after that does the instructor introduce the broader concepts of infodemic, mediated information crisis, bad actors, and professional codes of conduct.

The lesson should remain neutral. The instructor should not determine whether COVID moderation was ethical or unethical. The goal is to help students analyze stakeholders, harms, benefits, observability, control, authority, and how different frameworks make different ethical concerns visible.

---

# Weekly Topic
Applied Ethical Analysis Across Professional and Societal Frameworks

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students should be able to:

1. Analyze an ethically ambiguous socio-technical system without reducing the case to a single obvious judgment.
2. Distinguish false information, misinformation, disinformation, and malinformation.
3. Explain how “bad actor” can refer to malicious, negligent, reckless, strategic, institutional, or procedurally evasive behavior rather than only to an obviously malicious person.
4. Compare how professional and governance frameworks prioritize ethical concerns differently.
5. Identify stakeholders and evaluate how benefits, harms, authority, control, and observability are distributed across a system.
6. Recognize that professional codes of conduct help practitioners avoid becoming sources of harm through negligence, opacity, poor design, or failure to account for foreseeable misuse.

# Instructor Framing Notes

This week should not be framed as:

- “Was COVID moderation good or bad?”
- “Which ethical system is correct?”
- “Which society is morally superior?”
- “Which professional code gives the right answer?”

Instead, the lesson should emphasize:

- ethical weighting;
- mediated information crisis;
- bad actors and harmful agency;
- institutional assumptions;
- governance structures;
- stakeholder conflict;
- operational ethical ambiguity;
- and the separation between observability, control, and authority.

Students should recognize that all societies and professional frameworks blend deontological, consequentialist, and virtue-oriented tendencies, but they may weight these tendencies differently.

The discussion should remain focused on socio-technical systems relevant to data science rather than becoming a generalized political debate about pandemic policy.

# Class Session Structure

## Part 1 – Case Card and Opening Provocations
### Approximate Time: 20–25 minutes

The class begins with the short COVID-19 misinformation moderation case card. Students should read it silently and respond individually, preferably in their private journals, before discussion.

The instructor should stage the provocations one at a time. The goal is not to reach a final ethical verdict. The goal is to surface the criteria students already use when they evaluate an ambiguous socio-technical system.

### Provocation 1
Was COVID-19 misinformation moderation ethically justified?

Do not try to resolve the case completely. Identify what your answer depends on.

### Provocation 2
Imagine three possible versions of the same moderation pathway:

- the platform acts under its own policy;
- a public-health authority flags content and the platform acts;
- a regulator, court, or government body requires action.

Does your ethical evaluation change? If so, what changed: the stakeholder structure, the authority structure, the consequences of error, or your ethical criteria?

### Provocation 3
Which criteria did you use to make your judgment?

For example, did you emphasize public safety, individual freedom, fairness, transparency, institutional trust, technical reliability, professional responsibility, social stability, or proportionality?

### Provocation 4
Who has observability, control, and authority in this system?

Who can see what happened? Who can change the moderation rules or reverse a decision? Who has legitimate authority to define dangerous misinformation? Are observability, control, and authority aligned, or separated across different agents?

The instructor should avoid resolving the case and instead use student responses to reveal competing ethical weightings.

---

## Part 2 – Infodemic, Mediated Information Crisis, and Bad Actors
### Approximate Time: 25–30 minutes

After the opening provocations, the instructor introduces the idea of an *infodemic* and then generalizes it into the broader concept of a **mediated information crisis**.

A mediated information crisis occurs when information moves through technical, institutional, and social systems in ways that make it difficult for affected agents to determine what is true, who has authority, what action is appropriate, and who is responsible for the consequences.

The instructor should emphasize that this structure is not limited to health communication. Similar patterns occur in elections, climate change communication, war reporting, financial panic, disaster response, scientific controversies, public safety emergencies, and AI-generated media environments.

The instructor then introduces the concept of the **bad actor**.

In this course, a bad actor does not necessarily mean a bad person. A bad actor is an agent whose behavior degrades the integrity, safety, trust, fairness, or accountability of a system. This may occur through malice, negligence, recklessness, institutional incentives, strategic manipulation, or unexamined participation in harmful structures.

### Key Distinction

A system can produce harm without intent.

A bad actor can produce harm through intent.

A mediated system can amplify either one.

### False Information, Misinformation, Disinformation, and Malinformation

The instructor should distinguish:

- **False information**: information that is factually incorrect.
- **Misinformation**: false or misleading information shared without necessarily intending harm.
- **Disinformation**: false or misleading information shared deliberately to deceive, manipulate, profit, destabilize, or gain power.
- **Malinformation**: information that may be true or partially true but is used in a harmful, misleading, or contextually manipulative way.

The important point is that platforms may be able to observe a claim, but may not reliably observe the agency behind that claim.

### Same Claim, Different Agency

A false COVID-related claim may be:

- sincerely believed;
- socially reinforced;
- negligently amplified;
- strategically exploited;
- algorithmically amplified;
- institutionally tolerated;
- or deliberately manufactured.

This distinction prepares the transition to professional codes of conduct. Professional codes are not only tools for judging external bad actors. They are also tools for helping professionals avoid becoming bad actors through negligence, recklessness, opacity, poor design, or failure to account for foreseeable misuse.

## Part 3 – Professional Codes as Ethical Guardrails
### Approximate Time: 20 minutes

The instructor introduces the central idea of the second part of the class:

Professional codes operationalize ethical priorities as standards of conduct.

The lecture should not reteach ethical theory itself. Students are assumed to already understand deontology, consequentialism, and virtue ethics.

Instead, the instructor discusses how professional communities formalize expectations so that practitioners do not become sources of harm through ignorance, negligence, carelessness, institutional pressure, or bad-faith participation.

The transition question:

If bad actors may be malicious, negligent, reckless, or simply unreflective, what standards help professionals avoid becoming harmful actors within a system?

Topics may include:

- Professional ethics vs governance ethics
- Professional codes as self-governance
- Ethical systems as operational infrastructures
- Good-faith ambiguity vs bad-faith exploitation
- Individual responsibility vs institutional responsibility
- Why “be honest” is not enough when systems can be exploited by dishonest actors
- Why systems must be designed for both good-faith users and bad-faith manipulation

The instructor introduces ACM and IEEE at a high level.

### ACM High-Level Framing

ACM can be introduced as a computing and information-systems professional framework that foregrounds public good, avoidance of harm, privacy, fairness, transparency, honesty, competence, and professional responsibility.

### IEEE High-Level Framing

IEEE can be introduced as an engineering and technical-systems professional framework that foregrounds public safety, welfare, sustainability, technical honesty, risk disclosure, competence, conflicts of interest, and responsibility for technological consequences.

### Example of Possible Divergence Between ACM and IEEE

Provide one concrete example so students see that overlap does not mean identical evaluation.

Example:

A platform engineer knows that an automated COVID misinformation classifier has a substantially higher false-positive rate for certain dialect communities, but the company deploys it anyway because it reduces large volumes of dangerous false claims quickly.

- **ACM framing** may emphasize fairness, non-discrimination, transparency, and the professional obligation not to produce avoidable harm to already disadvantaged users.
- **IEEE framing** may emphasize safety, technical reliability, risk disclosure, and the obligation to communicate known system limitations before deployment.

Both frameworks may criticize the deployment, but the center of gravity differs. ACM may foreground inequity and harm to affected communities; IEEE may foreground engineering risk, insufficient testing, and reckless technical deployment.

### EU Governance Framing

EU-oriented governance frameworks may be introduced as broader institutional approaches that foreground oversight, proportionality, transparency, accountability, rights, and systemic risk.

## Part 4 – Ethical Framework Mapping Activity
### Approximate Time: 25–30 minutes

Students work in small groups.

Each group receives short instructor-selected excerpts or instructor-provided summaries from:

- ACM materials;
- IEEE ethics materials;
- and optionally a short EU governance excerpt.

These are introduced during class. Students are not expected to have read the full codes before this activity.

Groups identify:

- Which ethical tendencies appear most strongly weighted?
- What kinds of harms are foregrounded?
- What assumptions about professionals exist?
- What assumptions about institutions exist?
- How is responsibility distributed?
- What role does public welfare play?
- How are trust and accountability operationalized?
- How does the framework help prevent professional negligence, recklessness, opacity, or harmful participation?

Students are encouraged to identify mixtures of:

- deontological tendencies;
- consequentialist tendencies;
- virtue-oriented expectations;

rather than attempting to classify frameworks into rigid categories.

---

## Part 5 – Applied Ethical Analysis Activity
### Approximate Time: 45–60 minutes

Students return to the COVID-19 misinformation moderation case as the shared case. This shared case should be used before students move back to their own systems, because it allows the class to compare how different frameworks evaluate the same situation.

Students should not decide whether the moderation system was ethical or unethical at the beginning of the activity. They should first identify stakeholders, harms, benefits, agency, and power structure.

### Core Case Elements

The case includes:

- public-health authorities identifying harmful misinformation or providing guidance;
- platform companies designing and enforcing moderation policies;
- automated classifiers and human moderators applying rules;
- users posting or consuming COVID-related content;
- fact-checkers or expert reviewers participating in classification;
- researchers, regulators, courts, and the public attempting to understand or evaluate what happened;
- and different forms of harmful agency, from sincere misinformation to deliberate disinformation.

### Key Ethical Tensions

Students should consider:

- public health vs freedom of expression;
- expertise vs legitimate authority;
- platform control vs public accountability;
- harm reduction vs institutional trust;
- scientific uncertainty vs automated enforcement;
- global platform policy vs local cultural and legal context;
- sincere misinformation vs deliberate disinformation;
- user confusion vs strategic manipulation;
- professional responsibility vs adversarial exploitation;
- and whether systems should treat the same false claim differently depending on the agency behind it.

### Activity Procedure

Each group evaluates the same COVID-19 misinformation moderation case using:

- bad-actor analysis;
- ACM-oriented assumptions;
- IEEE-oriented assumptions;
- EU governance or risk/harm assumptions;
- BBS/EDOCA power-structure analysis.

Groups should identify:

- stakeholders;
- benefits;
- harms;
- different kinds of actors, including misinformed, negligent, reckless, strategic, malicious, institutional, and procedural actors;
- who has observability;
- who has control;
- who has authority;
- where those dimensions align;
- where those dimensions are separated;
- points of agreement between frameworks;
- points of conflict between frameworks;
- unresolved ambiguities.

Students should be encouraged to recognize that:

- multiple ethical conclusions may be defensible;
- frameworks may prioritize stakeholders differently;
- professional ethics and governance ethics may make different parts of the system visible;
- systems must be evaluated for both good-faith ambiguity and bad-faith exploitation;
- and compliance does not automatically resolve ethical ambiguity.

---

## Part 6 – BBS / EDOCA Reflection
### Approximate Time: 20 minutes

Students revisit the COVID-19 misinformation case through the lens of mediation and distributed responsibility.

Instructor framing:

> Professional frameworks identify values. EDOCA identifies power.

The purpose of this step is to determine whether ethical responsibility is aligned with actual observability, control, and authority.

Discussion prompts:

- Where does authority reside?
- Who controls the system?
- Who can observe system behavior?
- Which stakeholders are invisible to the system?
- Where are trust assumptions inserted?
- What forms of distortion may occur?
- Which ethical concerns emerge from mediation pathways themselves?
- How do institutional structures shape ethical outcomes?
- How do bad actors exploit observability gaps, trust gaps, or authority/control separation?

This portion reinforces earlier course concepts while demonstrating their applicability to professional ethical analysis.

---

## Part 7 – Closing Discussion and Synthesis
### Approximate Time: 15–20 minutes

Suggested concluding questions:

- Can globally deployed systems satisfy incompatible ethical expectations simultaneously?
- Are ethical frameworks culturally and institutionally situated?
- How should systems respond when they can observe a false claim but not the intent or agency behind that claim?
- When does legal compliance fail to resolve ethical responsibility?
- How do professional codes help practitioners avoid becoming sources of harm?
- Can computational systems ever be ethically neutral?
- How should data scientists operate when ethical frameworks conflict?

The instructor should avoid seeking final consensus. The objective is structured ethical reflection rather than closure.

# Suggested Follow-Up Assignment

## Comparative Ethical Framework Investigation

Students investigate how ACM, IEEE, and an EU-oriented governance framework differ in their ethical structure.

They use the COVID-19 misinformation moderation case as the required shared case so that the class can compare how the same system is evaluated through different frameworks.

Students should evaluate the system through:

- ACM-oriented ethics;
- IEEE-oriented ethics;
- one broader governance or societal perspective, such as EU Trustworthy AI, EU AI Act risk/harm framing, or platform governance;
- bad-actor analysis;
- and BBS/EDOCA power-structure analysis.

Students must:

1. Identify stakeholders.
2. Describe competing ethical priorities.
3. Distinguish between misinformation, disinformation, negligence, recklessness, and strategic bad-faith behavior where relevant.
4. Analyze differing ethical conclusions.
5. Identify who has observability, control, and authority.
6. Discuss whether those dimensions are aligned or separated.
7. Discuss unresolved ambiguities.
8. Reflect on how professional codes help prevent practitioners or institutions from becoming sources of harm.
9. Reflect on how differing institutional assumptions shape ethical evaluation.
10. Reference mediation structures where appropriate.

Students should not attempt to determine a single “correct” answer. Instead, they should demonstrate the ability to analyze how differing ethical systems operationalize responsibility and prioritize competing concerns.

# Instructor Notes

The most important pedagogical objective this week is helping students recognize that:

- ethical systems are themselves designed systems;
- professional frameworks operationalize ethical assumptions differently;
- frameworks make some harms visible and obscure others;
- bad actors may be malicious, negligent, reckless, strategic, institutional, or procedurally evasive;
- professional codes help practitioners avoid becoming sources of harm;
- and globally distributed computational systems frequently operate across incompatible ethical expectations.

Students should leave the lesson understanding that ethical reasoning in professional practice often involves navigating tensions between:

- professional obligations;
- institutional structures;
- governance systems;
- stakeholder needs;
- legal frameworks;
- societal values;
- misinformation and disinformation;
- adversarial behavior;
- observability;
- control;
- and authority.

This lesson serves as a bridge into later weeks focused on adaptive ethical reasoning, uncertainty, and the development of personal ethical frameworks.
