## Slide 5 – Provocation 1

### Text on Slide
Was COVID-19 misinformation moderation ethically justified?

Do not try to resolve the case completely.

Identify what your answer depends on.

### Framing Notes
Students answer individually before discussion. Strong responses should identify conditions rather than jump to yes/no conclusions. Look for criteria such as public safety, free expression, scientific uncertainty, authority, transparency, appeal, proportionality, and trust.

---

## Slide 6 – Provocation 2

### Text on Slide
Imagine three versions of the same moderation pathway:

1. The platform acts under its own policy.
2. A public-health authority flags content and the platform acts.
3. A regulator, court, or government body requires action.

Does your ethical evaluation change?

### Framing Notes
Separate action from authority. The same visible outcome—labeling, downranking, removal, or suspension—may have a different ethical interpretation depending on who initiated it, who authorized it, and who controls the pathway.

---

## Slide 7 – Provocation 3

### Text on Slide
What changed?

Stakeholders?

Authority?

Consequences of error?

Your ethical criteria?

### Framing Notes
Continue Provocation 2 by asking students to name the variable that changed. Did the system change, or did the context change? Did the moral weight of harm change? Did public authority become more legitimate or more dangerous? Did responsibility become clearer or more distributed?

## Slide 9 – Provocation 5

Observability?

Control?

Authority?

## Slide 12 – Infodemic

### Text on Slide
Infodemic

Too much information, including false or misleading information, during a disease outbreak.

The problem is not only false information.

The problem is overload, uncertainty, speed, trust, and authority.

### Framing Notes
Introduce WHO’s term at a high level. The definition should be enough for class use; detailed citations can live in the case study. Emphasize that an infodemic includes true, false, outdated, uncertain, confusing, and deliberately manipulative information.

---

## Slide 13 – Mediated Information Crisis

### Text on Slide
Mediated Information Crisis

Information moves through technical, institutional, and social systems in ways that make it difficult to determine:

What is true?
Who has authority?
What action is appropriate?
Who is responsible?

### Framing Notes
This is the generalized concept. It connects the case directly to BBS. Signals move through multiple sources, vectors, destinations, institutions, platforms, and communities. Distortion and trust failures occur across mediation pathways.

---

## Slide 14 – From Information Crisis to Agency

### Text on Slide
Information does not move by itself.

Actors create, share, suppress, amplify, classify, and interpret signals.

Some act in good faith.

Some act carelessly.

Some act strategically.

Some act maliciously.

### Framing Notes
Pivot from environment to agency. Misinformation is not one ethical category. The same false claim can be carried by different kinds of actors with different degrees of awareness, responsibility, and intent.

---

## Slide 15 – False Information, Misinformation, Disinformation, Malinformation

### Text on Slide
False information  
Factually incorrect

Misinformation  
False or misleading, not necessarily intended to harm

Disinformation  
False or misleading, deliberately used to deceive or manipulate

Malinformation  
True or partly true information used harmfully or out of context

### Framing Notes
Keep the taxonomy concise. Students need enough vocabulary to distinguish sincere error from deliberate manipulation. This distinction prepares the bad actor discussion.

---

## Slide 16 – Same Claim, Different Agency

### Text on Slide
The same false claim may be:

Sincerely believed
Socially reinforced
Negligently amplified
Strategically exploited
Algorithmically amplified
Institutionally tolerated
Deliberately manufactured

### Framing Notes
This is a central slide. The ethical meaning of a signal depends partly on the agency behind it. However, platforms may see the claim without seeing the actor’s intent or social context.

---

## Slide 17 – The Platform’s Problem

### Text on Slide
A platform may observe the claim.

It may not observe the agency behind the claim.

It may control visibility.

It may not know intent.

It may act at scale before context is clear.

### Framing Notes
Connect observability and control. A system may have high operational control but low contextual observability. The system may treat sincere misinformation and strategic disinformation the same way, or fail to respond to coordinated manipulation.

---

## Slide 18 – Bad Actor

### Text on Slide
Bad Actor

An agent whose behavior degrades the integrity, safety, trust, fairness, or accountability of a system.

A bad actor is not always a bad person.

### Framing Notes
Define the term operationally rather than morally. The goal is not accusation. The goal is to identify harmful agency in a system.

---

## Slide 19 – Bad Actor Spectrum

### Text on Slide
Malicious

Strategic

Reckless

Negligent

Misinformed

Institutional

Procedural

### Framing Notes
Briefly define each category orally. Malicious actors intend harm or manipulation. Strategic actors exploit systems for advantage. Reckless actors ignore foreseeable risk. Negligent actors fail professional standards of care. Misinformed actors sincerely believe false claims. Institutional bad actors create harmful incentive structures. Procedural bad actors exploit rules while undermining their purpose.

---

## Slide 20 – Why This Matters

### Text on Slide
Systems must be designed for:

Good-faith ambiguity

and

Bad-faith exploitation

### Framing Notes
Bridge to professional codes. Ethical systems cannot assume everyone is acting responsibly, but they also cannot treat every mistake as malicious. Good design must handle uncertainty, error, misuse, abuse, incentives, and accountability.

---

## Slide 21 – The Professional Problem

### Text on Slide
How do professionals avoid becoming sources of harm?

Through:

Standards
Responsibility
Disclosure
Documentation
Testing
Transparency
Accountability

### Framing Notes
Shift bad actor discussion toward self-governance. Professional codes are tools for preventing practitioners, teams, and institutions from drifting into negligence, recklessness, opacity, or harmful complicity.

---

## Slide 22 – Professional Codes as Guardrails

### Text on Slide
Professional codes operationalize ethical priorities as standards of conduct.

They help define what responsible practice requires.

They do not eliminate ambiguity.

They constrain it.

### Framing Notes
Formal transition to ACM and IEEE. Professional codes do not solve ethics. They provide shared criteria that make professional responsibility more explicit and repeatable.

## Slide 26 – Three Levels of Ethical Structure

### Text on Slide
Professional ethics
What should responsible practitioners do?

Governance ethics

Mediation analysis
Who can observe, control, and authorize action?

### Framing Notes
Synthesize the layers. ACM and IEEE speak strongly to professional conduct. EU frameworks speak more to governance and systemic accountability. BBS/EDOCA describes how power and responsibility move through the system.

## Slide 27 – Frameworks Identify Values

### Text on Slide
Frameworks identify values.

EDOCA identifies power.

Values ask:
What matters?

Power asks:
Who can act?
Who can see?
Who can authorize?

### Framing Notes
Emphasize this distinction. It gives students a clear reason to keep using BBS/EDOCA alongside ethical codes. A code may say transparency matters, but EDOCA asks who can actually observe the relevant transition.

## Slide 31 – Observability, Control, Authority

### Text on Slide
Who can see the decision?

Who can change the system?

Who can authorize action?

Who is affected but cannot intervene?

### Framing Notes
Return explicitly to EDOCA. For the COVID case, public-health authorities may have expertise, platforms may have operational control, regulators may have formal authority, and users may have limited observability. The system is ethically complicated because these roles do not fully align.

## Slide 32 – Ethical Ambiguity

### Text on Slide
The question is not:

Was moderation right or wrong?

The question is:

How do different frameworks assign responsibility, define harm, and manage uncertainty?

### Framing Notes
Prevent premature closure. Students are not being asked to arrive at a final moral verdict. They are being asked to demonstrate structured ethical analysis.
