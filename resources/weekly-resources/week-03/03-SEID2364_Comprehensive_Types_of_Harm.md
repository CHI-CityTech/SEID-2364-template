

# **SEID 2364 — Taxonomy of Sociotechnical harms**

## Consolidated core and extended harm categories for ethical analysis and class discussion

Dr. David B. Smith

2026-03-10

**Purpose.** This document consolidates a large number of different types of harm. It is intended as a reference for identifying, naming, and discussing harms in complex socio‑technical systems. The categories are intentionally overlapping; real cases often involve multiple harms occurring simultaneously or cascading over time.

**Note on trust:** Loss of trust can be both a consequence of other harms and a direct, systemic harm in its own right. Trust is foundational to the functioning of sociotechnical systems—loss of trust in platforms, processes, information integrity, or institutions can undermine system effectiveness, safety, and social cohesion. For this reason, trust harm is included as a distinct analytic category and may also appear as a cross-cutting theme in other harms.

* **Trust harm:** Erosion or loss of trust in systems, platforms, processes, information, or institutions. This includes failures of reliability, transparency, or accountability that undermine confidence, create uncertainty, or reduce willingness to participate, rely on, or cooperate with sociotechnical systems. Loss of trust can have cascading effects, amplifying other harms and destabilizing social or technical relationships.

# **1\. Core harms** 

* **Physical harm:** Injury, illness, or threats to bodily safety and bodily autonomy, including unsafe conditions or physical constraint.  
* **Psychological harm:** Negative impacts on mental and emotional well‑being, such as stress, anxiety, trauma, fear, or loss of perceived control.  
* **Economic harm:** Financial loss or reduced economic opportunity, including fees, lost wages, denial of work, increased costs, or reduced access to resources.  
* **Reputational harm:** Damage to how others perceive a person or organization, including loss of credibility, status, or trust due to labels, errors, or disclosures.  
* **Social harm:** Harms to relationships, community participation, belonging, and social cohesion, including exclusion, stigma, harassment, or polarization.  
* **Institutional harm:** Harms arising from organizations or governance systems, such as unfair procedures, loss of due process, weakened accountability, or degraded service quality.

# **2\. Extended harms (analytic expansion)**

## A. Harms to Cognition & Information (Epistemic/Attention) {#a.-harms-to-cognition-&-information-(epistemic/attention)}

* **Epistemic harm:** Distortion of what people can know or understand, including misinformation, biased knowledge production, and opaque or misleading systems.  
* **Attention harm:** Capture, fragmentation, or depletion of attention that undermines focus, autonomy, or well‑being.  
* **Automation bias:** Over‑reliance on system outputs at the expense of human judgment, critical thinking, or contextual understanding.  
* **Deskilling harm:** Erosion of human expertise or capability over time due to over‑automation or loss of practice.

## B. Harms to Autonomy & Dignity (Respect/Process) {#b.-harms-to-autonomy-&-dignity-(respect/process)}

* **Dignitary harm:** Harm to a person’s inherent dignity, including being treated as less worthy, dehumanized, coerced, or denied agency and respectful consideration.  
* **Consent erosion:** Lack of meaningful informed consent, transparency, or ability to opt out of systems or data use.  
* **Moral distress:** Psychological and ethical strain experienced when individuals are required to participate in or rely on systems they believe are wrong.  
* **Surveillance creep:** Gradual expansion of monitoring, data collection, or control beyond original intent or justification.

## C. Systemic & Intertemporal Harms (Structure/Time) {#c.-systemic-&-intertemporal-harms-(structure/time)}

* **Distributional harm:** Unequal allocation of harms and benefits across populations, often reinforcing or amplifying existing inequalities.  
* **Lock‑in harm:** Early technical or policy choices that constrain future alternatives and make harmful paths difficult to reverse.  
* **Path‑dependency harm:** Systems shaping future decisions, norms, or infrastructures in ways that limit later choice or reform.  
* **Precarity harm:** Increased instability or insecurity in work, income, housing, or access to essential resources.  
* **Opportunity foreclosure:** Removal or narrowing of pathways that would otherwise be available to individuals or groups.  
* **Deferred harm:** Negative consequences that materialize only later, often outside the initial deployment context.  
* **Intergenerational harm:** Shifting risks, costs, or reduced options onto future populations.  
* **Accountability harm:** Unclear, diffused, or obscured responsibility for outcomes, making redress or correction difficult.

# **3\. Note on overlap and cascades**

**Key idea.** These harm categories are not mutually exclusive. A single system action often produces a cascade of harms across levels (individual, group, organization, society) and across time. Ethical analysis involves identifying both direct harms and downstream or indirect harms, even when they are less visible or delayed.
