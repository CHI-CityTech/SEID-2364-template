# Assignment 9: Incident Selection Guide

**Purpose**: Help you identify and evaluate potential incidents for the EDOCA mediation pathway analysis in Assignment 9.

This guide is *not* a list of incidents you must use. Rather, it's a framework for finding timely, well-documented incidents that meet the analytical requirements of the assignment.

---

## What Makes a Good Incident?

An incident suitable for Assignment 9 should have these characteristics:

### 1. **Multi-Agent Involvement**
- At least 3 distinct agents (not a single person or decision)
- Agents from different categories: human, computational, institutional, and/or infrastructural
- Pathways that show how harm flows between agents

**Why**: Single-person or isolated events cannot reveal system mediation. You need to trace how agency, information, and responsibility flow across boundaries.

**Example domains**:
- AI systems deployed in institutional contexts (healthcare, hiring, criminal justice, finance)
- Algorithmic systems at scale (recommendations, content moderation, content amplification)
- Data breaches or misuse (who collected, who stored, who accessed, who was harmed)
- Autonomous or semi-autonomous systems (vehicles, manufacturing, robots in public space)
- Human-AI collaboration failures (where the system was supposed to assist but created harm)

### 2. **Clear Documentation**
- Published reports, news articles, academic analyses, or regulatory investigations
- Sufficient detail to reconstruct what happened, who was involved, and how harm occurred
- Primary or secondary sources you can cite

**Why**: You cannot invent the incident details. You must reconstruct what actually occurred from available evidence.

**Where to find documented incidents**:
- Academic papers on AI harm, bias, or system failures
- News articles from reputable outlets (AP, Reuters, NYT, BBC, ProPublica, etc.)
- Government reports (NTSB, FTC, SEC, congressional testimony)
- Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) releases
- Regulatory findings or settlement documents
- Industry research labs and ethics teams (Google AI, Microsoft Research, etc.)
- Tech policy organizations (AI Now Institute, Center for AI Safety, etc.)
- Incident databases (AI Incident Database, AlgorithmWatch, etc.)

### 3. **Traceable Mediation Pathways**
- You can identify where information moved between agents
- You can name decisions or transformations that occurred
- You can describe at least 2–3 distinct pathways (data flow, decision flow, accountability flow)
- You can identify at least 2 transitions where you can apply EDOCA

**Why**: The assignment asks you to reconstruct mediation. If a system is opaque or poorly documented, you cannot complete the analysis rigorously.

**Test**: Can you answer these for your incident?
- Who collected or created the initial information?
- How did that information move to decision-makers?
- What transformations occurred?
- Who could see the results?
- Who was affected but could not see what was happening?
- Where did control and authority reside?
- Where did responsibility become unclear?

### 4. **Connection to Course Frameworks**
- You can meaningfully apply concepts from Assignments 1–8
- You can use BBS language (agents, mediation pathways, physical/virtual space, SVD)
- You can analyze through at least one ethical framework from Assignment 4
- You can identify systems structures (actors, determinants, information flows) as in Assignment 5

**Why**: The goal is to synthesize, not start over. Your analysis should build on tools you've already developed.

### 5. **Analyzable Scope**
- Complex enough to require multi-pathway analysis
- Bounded enough to analyze in 3–5 pages with depth
- Not so recent that documentation is unavailable or incomplete

**Why**: Too simple = shallow analysis. Too complex or undocumented = impossible to complete rigorously.

**Test**: Can you identify the core agents and primary pathways within 1–2 hours of research?

## What NOT to Select

### ❌ Single-Point Failures
- "The doctor made a wrong diagnosis"
- "An AI chatbot gave bad advice to one person"
- "Someone mistyped a formula"

**Why**: These are individual errors, not system mediation failures. Assignment 9 requires you to show how harm emerged through a *system*.

### ❌ Purely Intentional Harm or Fraud
- "A company deliberately lied to consumers"
- "A person knowingly committed a crime"
- "Someone hacked a system maliciously"

**Why**: Assignment 9 focuses on capability-vs-intent mismatches and how systems produce unintended outcomes. Deliberate wrongdoing doesn't require the same analysis.

### ❌ Hypothetical or Speculative Incidents
- "What if a self-driving car failed?"
- "Imagine an AI system that..."
- Science fiction scenarios

**Why**: You need real documented evidence. Use case studies only if they're explicitly framed as scenarios in published materials (like course case studies).

### ❌ Incidents With No Documentation
- Rumors or anecdotes you heard
- Personal experiences without public records
- Incidents that only one source has reported without verification

**Why**: You cannot reconstruct pathways without evidence. Cite sources.

### ❌ Current Events Still Unfolding
- Incidents from fewer than 3–5 days ago without investigation/reporting
- Situations where details are still changing daily
- Incidents under active legal proceedings with sealed documents

**Why**: You need enough documentation to understand what happened, not speculation about what might have happened.

## How to Evaluate an Incident You've Found

Once you've identified a potential incident, ask yourself:

1. **Do I have enough documentation to answer "What happened?"** (1–2 pages of narrative, no analysis)
   - If yes: ✓ Pass
   - If no: Find a better-documented incident

2. **Can I identify at least 3 distinct agents?**
   - If yes: ✓ Pass
   - If no: This might be too simple; find a more complex incident

3. **Can I trace at least 2 major mediation pathways?**
   - If yes: ✓ Pass
   - If no: This might be too linear; find a system with multiple flows

4. **Can I apply EDOCA to at least 2 transitions?**
   - If yes: ✓ Pass
   - If no: This might not have enough mediation complexity

5. **Can I connect this to something from Assignments 1–8?**
   - If yes: ✓ Pass
   - If no: Consider finding an incident in a domain you've already analyzed

6. **Is the incident recent enough to feel relevant but old enough to be well-documented?**
   - Recent: Within the last 1–3 years (allows for timely analysis)
   - Documented: At least 3–6 months of public reporting available
   - If yes: ✓ Pass

## Example: How to Vet an Incident

**Incident**: "Facial recognition misidentification leads to wrongful arrest"

**Check 1 — Documentation?**
- ✓ Yes: NIST studies, FBI reports, multiple news investigations

**Check 2 — Multiple agents?**
- ✓ Yes: AI system vendor, law enforcement, courts, affected individuals, infrastructure

**Check 3 — Mediation pathways?**
- ✓ Yes: 
  - Data pathway: image → algorithm → match score
  - Decision pathway: match score → law enforcement decision → arrest
  - Accountability pathway: (broken—individuals often unaware they were identified)

**Check 4 — EDOCA applicability?**
- ✓ Yes: 
  - Transition 1: Image input → algorithm output (Effort: computational; Distortion: high false-positive rates for certain demographics; Observability: law enforcement can see score, suspects cannot; Control: vendor sets algorithm, law enforcement interprets; Authority: both vendor and police, no explicit override mechanism)
  - Transition 2: Match score → arrest decision (Effort: human judgment; Distortion: score is treated as evidence; Observability: suspect unaware of algorithm's role; Control: police decision; Authority: police discretion, no constraint from algorithm designers)

**Check 5 — Connection to course?**
- ✓ Yes: Relates to bias (Assignment 8), systems structure (Assignment 5), regulatory oversight (Assignment 7), harm identification (Assignment 3)

**Check 6 — Timing and scope?**
- ✓ Yes: Incidents documented since ~2020; sufficient public reporting; bounded enough for analysis

**Verdict**: ✓ Good incident choice.

## Final Advice

1. **Start with your own curiosity**: What recent news about AI or data systems made you uncomfortable or curious? That's a good starting point.

2. **Work backwards from documentation**: If you find a great article, follow its citations to deeper sources and official reports.

3. **Don't wait until the last minute**: Good incident research takes time. Start looking 1–2 weeks before the assignment is due.

4. **Talk to peers**: Other students will be researching different incidents. Compare notes in study groups—seeing how classmates apply the same frameworks to different systems will deepen your own analysis.

5. **Cite everything**: Even if you don't use a source directly in your final analysis, cite it in a brief "Research Consulted" section. This shows your work and helps future students.

6. **Remember the goal**: You're not arguing whether the incident was good or bad. You're using it as a case study to understand *how* systems produce harm through mediation pathways, and *where* responsibility becomes distributed or unclear.

---

## TL;DR

- Find a **real, documented incident** involving **multiple agents** and **traceable mediation**
- Choose something **recent enough to be interesting** but **old enough to be well-understood**
- Make sure it has **clear pathways to analyze** with **EDOCA tools**
- Cite your sources
- Good luck
