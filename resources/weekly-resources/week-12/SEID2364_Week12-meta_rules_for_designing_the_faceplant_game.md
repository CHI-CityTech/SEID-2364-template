# V1 – SEID 2364 Meta-Rules
## Designing the Faceplant COVID-19 Misinformation Game

## Purpose

This document defines the **meta-rules** for the Faceplant COVID-19 misinformation game-specification activity. These are not yet the final rules of the game. They are the conditions students should use while developing the game rules, stakeholder roles, signal pathways, and scoring system.

The activity is itself a design problem. Students are not simply playing a finished game; they are constructing a bounded model of an ethical system.

## 1. The Game Is Built Before It Is Played

The first task is to define the game space. Students must decide what exists in the system before judging whether an outcome is ethical or successful.

The game requires at least:

- a society value profile;
- stakeholder cards;
- spaces;
- signals;
- vectors or pathways;
- actions or transformations;
- outcome profiles;
- a method for scoring alignment between outcomes and the society profile.

If an element is not defined, it cannot be used as evidence in the game.

## 2. Society Defines the Win Condition

The society profile must be defined before outcomes can be judged.

A society is represented by its position on four value axes:

- Collective Protection ↔ Individual Freedom
- Institutional Trust ↔ Institutional Constraint
- Information Integrity ↔ Open Expression
- Economic Continuity ↔ Protective Intervention

Different societies may define different wins because they prioritize different values. The same stakeholder action may be successful in one society and unacceptable in another.

## 3. Stakeholders Do Not Share the Society Goal Automatically

Each stakeholder has its own operational goal, authority, and strategic pressure. These may support, complicate, or conflict with the society goal.

A stakeholder card should establish:

- what the stakeholder wants;
- what the stakeholder is allowed or empowered to do;
- what the stakeholder can do to signals, vectors, or spaces;
- what the stakeholder fears or wants to avoid;
- where the stakeholder’s goal may conflict with the society win condition.

Stakeholders are not assumed to be morally aligned with society. They may be aligned, partially aligned, indifferent, conflicted, opportunistic, or adversarial.

## 4. Faceplant Is a Persistent External Actor

Faceplant exists across all society profiles. It does not disappear when society values change.

However, Faceplant’s legitimacy, risk, and viable strategy change depending on the society in which it operates. A platform action that appears responsible in one society may appear coercive, costly, weak, or illegitimate in another.

Faceplant’s likely operational goal is to preserve long-term platform value, profitability, user retention, engagement, market position, and legitimacy while managing legal, reputational, regulatory, and public-health risks.

## 5. Signals Must Be Tracked as They Move

Signals are not static objects. A signal may change every time it moves through a stakeholder, space, vector, or platform process.

A signal can be:

- generated;
- amplified;
- suppressed;
- delayed;
- reframed;
- distorted;
- verified;
- challenged;
- authorized;
- monetized;
- personalized;
- routed to a new audience.

Whenever a signal moves, the class should record what changed.

## 6. Spaces Change Access Conditions

A signal’s ethical meaning depends partly on the space it occupies.

Different spaces create different conditions of:

- observability;
- control;
- authority;
- effort;
- distortion;
- cost;
- trust;
- visibility;
- timing.

For example, the same COVID claim may behave differently in an official public-health briefing, a personal group chat, an influencer video, a recommender system, a regulatory hearing, or a corporate email thread.

Therefore, EDOCA should usually be evaluated dynamically as the signal moves through spaces, rather than treated as fixed on a stakeholder card.

## 7. Authority Must Be Explicit

Authority is not the same as control.

An actor may be able to do something technically without having legitimate authority to do it. Another actor may have formal authority but limited practical control or observability.

The game should distinguish:

- who can act;
- who is allowed to act;
- who can override an action;
- who can challenge an action;
- who is accountable for the action.

Authority can belong to stakeholders, institutions, spaces, laws, platform rules, professional standards, or social trust.

## 8. Outcomes Are Scored by Alignment With Society Values

An outcome should be assigned an outcome profile using the same value axes as the society profile.

The outcome profile is then compared to the society profile.

Use the labels:

- SL = Strong Left
- LL = Leans Left
- N = Neutral
- LR = Leans Right
- SR = Strong Right

The outcome score is calculated by counting the distance between the society profile and the outcome profile on each axis.

Suggested penalty system:

- 0 = exact match / full alignment
- -1 = one step away
- -2 = two steps away
- -3 = three steps away
- -4 = four steps away

With four axes, the best possible society score is 0. The worst possible society score is -16.

## 9. Least-Negotiable Values Can Override Total Score

A society may define a least-negotiable value. If an outcome violates that value, the society may reject the outcome even if the total score is relatively high.

This prevents the game from treating all values as interchangeable. Some societies may accept trade-offs. Others may define threshold conditions that cannot be crossed.

## 10. Costs Must Be Counted

Mitigation is not free. The game should consider cost as part of system design.

Costs may include:

- financial cost;
- labor cost;
- technical cost;
- moderation cost;
- legal or compliance cost;
- reputational cost;
- opportunity cost;
- public trust cost;
- cost of delay;
- cost of inaction.

Costs may be paid by different actors. The game should ask who pays, who benefits, and who is burdened.

## 11. The Internal Game and Meta-Game Must Be Distinguished

The internal game includes the defined rules, actions, cards, pathways, and scoring procedures.

The meta-game includes the larger environment that shapes or exploits the internal game:

- market competition;
- regulation;
- public pressure;
- reputation;
- platform migration;
- hidden coordination;
- enforcement gaps;
- media attention;
- political incentives;
- professional standards.

Many ethical failures occur when actors exploit the difference between the internal game and the meta-game.

## 12. The Game Should Preserve Ethical Ambiguity

The activity should not collapse into simple good/bad judgments. A good move for one stakeholder may be harmful for society. A good outcome for one society may be unacceptable for another.

The rules should preserve:

- conflicting goals;
- incomplete information;
- asymmetrical authority;
- strategic behavior;
- unintended consequences;
- delayed effects;
- ambiguous responsibility;
- competing definitions of success.

If every answer is obvious, the game is not modeling the ethical problem well enough.

## 13. The Rules May Be Revised During Play

Because the class is designing the game, rule revision is part of the activity.

A rule may need revision if:

- it makes an outcome impossible to evaluate;
- it hides an important ethical tension;
- it treats unlike cases as the same;
- it gives one actor unrealistic power;
- it makes stakeholder goals too simple;
- it fails to account for costs or authority;
- it prevents meaningful disagreement.

Rule changes should be recorded as part of the design process.

## 14. The Goal Is Not to Finish the Game

The goal of the activity is not necessarily to complete a polished game in one class session. The goal is to practice ethical system design.

A successful class session may produce:

- a partial society profile;
- a stakeholder map;
- one signal pathway;
- a revised rule;
- an unresolved conflict;
- a clearer account of what information is missing;
- a better understanding of why the system is ethically difficult.

The activity succeeds when students can explain how values, stakeholders, signals, spaces, authority, and incentives interact to produce ethical outcomes.

## 15. Core Design Question

At every stage, the class should return to the central question:

**Given this society’s values, what rules, pathways, authorities, and stakeholder actions would produce the strongest society win condition while preserving ethical ambiguity and avoiding unacceptable trade-offs?**
